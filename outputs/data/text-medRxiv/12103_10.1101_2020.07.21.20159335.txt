Abstract:

19

New Zealand had 1499 cases of COVID-19 before eliminating transmission of the

20

virus. Extensive contract tracing during the outbreak has resulted in a dataset of

21

epidemiologically linked cases. This data contains useful information about the

22

transmission dynamics of the virus, its dependence on factors such as age, and its

23

response to different control measures.

24

We use Monte-Carlo network construction techniques to provide an estimate of the

25

number of secondary cases for every individual infected during the outbreak. We then

26

apply standard statistical techniques to quantify differences between groups of

27

individuals.

28

Children under 10 years old are significantly under-represented in the case data.

29

Children infected fewer people on average and had a lower secondary attack rate in

30

comparison to adults and the elderly. Imported cases infected fewer people on

31

average and had a lower secondary attack rate than domestically acquired cases.

32

Superspreading is a significant contributor to the epidemic dynamics, with 20% of

33

cases among adults responsible for 65-85% of transmission. Asymptomatic cases

34

infected fewer individuals than clinical cases. Serial intervals are approximately

35

normally distributed (ğœ‡ = 5.0 days, ğœ = 5.7 days). Early isolation and quarantine of

36

cases reduced secondary transmission rates.

37

Border controls and strong social distancing measures, particularly when targeted at

38

superspreading, play a significant role in reducing the spread of COVID-19.

Introduction

41

Between 26 February and 22 May 2020, New Zealand recorded 1499 confirmed and

42

probable cases of COVID-19 (Figure 1). Of these, 575 had a recent history of overseas

43

travel. With the exception of international arrivals, which are subject to mandatory 14-

44

day quarantine, no community cases were reported from 22 May to 10 August. This

45

means that New Zealand has a high probability of having eliminated community

46

transmission during this period.

On 19 March, gatherings of more than 100 people were banned and, on 21 March, a

58

four-level alert level system was introduced. Alert level 1 was effectively business as

59

usual but with border restrictions. New Zealand entered alert level 2 on 21 March, level

60

3 on 23 March, and level 4 on 26 March. Alert level 4 included strict stay-at-home

61

measures, and all non-essential business and schools were closed. The country

62

remained at alert level 4 until 28 April, when it moved to level 3 allowing partial school

63

openings and non-essential businesses to operate under strict non-contact guidelines.

64

On 14 May, New Zealand moved to alert level 2 and most schools and businesses

65

reopened but large gatherings were still banned. On 9 June, the country moved back

66

to alert level 1 and a strict testing programme for international arrivals was established

67

to complement the ongoing 14-day mandatory quarantine.

68

New Zealand also has a widespread testing regime that quickly encouraged testing of

69

anyone with even minor symptoms. Up to 5 June (2 weeks after the last reported

70

domestic case on 22 May), 291,994 tests were performed (58 tests per 1000 people)

71

with a positivity rate of around 0.4% (Ministry of Health, 2020). Since mid-June, all

72

international arrivals are tested on day three and day twelve, and are not permitted to

73

leave their 14-day mandatory quarantine before returning a negative test result.

In this paper, we examine the epidemic transmission tree in New Zealand using

81

detailed case data and contact tracing information. This is an unusual dataset because

82

it describes a closed outbreak in which there are no ongoing transmission chains, and

83

many cases are epidemiologically linked to a likely index case. Because not all cases

84

have a potential index case identified and some cases have multiple potential index

85

cases, the transmission tree is not completely or uniquely defined. We reconstruct

86

multiple instances of the transmission tree using a Monte-Carlo technique. This

87

approach assigns a randomly selected index case to each secondary case based on

88

the dates of symptom onset, cluster identification, and the results of contact tracing

89

investigations. We assume a fixed proportion of infections were undetected because

90

they were asymptomatic, paucisymptomatic, or for some other reason.

91

From this suite of generated transmission trees, we calculate the individual

92

reproduction number ğ‘…ğ‘– for each infected individual and explore the distribution of ğ‘…ğ‘–

93

and the secondary attack rate at different points in the epidemic timeline. We explore

94

how these quantities vary for different age groups and types of case, imported or

95

domestic, and investigate the effect of early case isolation. We also investigate the

96

degree of superspreading in the transmission dynamics. Although some assumptions

97

are required to allow for missing or noisy data, our results are not model-dependent,

98

unlike most estimates of effective reproduction number for COVID-19 [2] [3, 4]. Our

99

sample size is larger than previous studies that have estimated the secondary attack

100

rate (e.g. Bi, Wu (5), Ganyani, Kremer (6), Cheng, Jian (7)). This allows for more

101

reliable estimates and for an investigation of how secondary attack rate varies with

102

age. We also reconstruct the entire transmission tree rather than just estimating of the

103

serial interval distribution. This provides a richer source of information about the

Incidence by age

222

We investigated COVID-19 incidence by age by splitting the clinical cases into three

223

age groups: under 10 years, 0-65 years, and over 65 years. We consider domestic

224

and imported cases separately and further split the data by significant time periods:

225

before and after alert level 4 was introduced (26 March) for the domestic cases; and

226

during three distinct periods of border control for imported cases: no restrictions

227

(before 16 March), self-isolation (16 March to 9 April) and government-mandated

228

quarantine (after 9 April).

229

Children under 10 are significantly under-represented in the case data relative to the

230

New Zealand population demographic of 12.7% (Figure 3). The domestic cases (Fig

231

3 A,B) contain relatively more children under 10 than the imported cases and these

232

infections predominantly occurred during alert level 4 (2.6% of cases were in children

233

before 26 March, 4.4% on or after 26 March). During alert level 4, schools and day

234

care centres were closed so children are likely to have spent more time in family

235

settings where attack rates are higher [5] resulting in a higher incidence for this group.

236

Of the 576 imported cases (Fig 3 C-E), only 2 were in children under 10 years old.

237

Both these cases were connected with an imported adult case with the same travel

238

history and symptom onset 4 to 7 days prior.

246

The distribution of serial intervals (Figure 4) resulting from the Monte Carlo

247

reconstruction contained a substantial number of outliers. It is likely these correspond

248

to cases that were infected by an undetected case, or the index case was wrongly

249

251

Discussion

396

We analysed the transmission dynamics of COVID-19 using a comprehensive dataset

397

for a completed outbreak. Our analysis investigated the role of age and type of case

398

(imported or domestic) on reproduction number, secondary attack rate, likelihood of

399

superspreading. We also investigated the effect of various control measures, including

400

case isolation, population-wide restrictions, and self-isolation or quarantine of

401

international arrivals.

402

We found that children under 10 years contribute less to the spread of COVID-19 than

403

other age groups. Children under 10 infected fewer people on average, had a lower

404

secondary attack rate, and were less likely to be superspreaders. This is consistent

405

with existing evidence on the role of children in COVID-19 transmission [15, 16].

406

The finding that superspreading is a significant contributor to transmission is

407

consistent with existing empirical and modelling studies [17] [18] [19]. Our results show

408

that among adults 20% of cases are responsible for between 65% and 85% of

409

transmission. This suggests that interventions targeting superspreaders or

410

superspreading events may be particularly effective in reducing the spread of COVID-

411

19. These may include restrictions on gathering size, particularly in closed

412

environments or crowded spaces [17].

413

In the dataset we used to reconstruct epidemic transmission tree, the index case is

414

either missing or not uniquely identified in some cases. It is also likely there were

415

asymptomatic or paucisymptomatic cases that were undetected. We therefore used

416

Monte Carlo methods to reconstruct missing cases and missing transmission routes

417

(nodes and links respectively in the epidemic tree). It is possible that phylodynamic

418

data on the genetic sequence of the virus recovered from positive test results could
