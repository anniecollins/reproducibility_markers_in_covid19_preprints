Abstract

26

We simulated epidemic projections of a potential COVID-19 outbreak in a university population

27

of 38,000 persons, under varying combinations of mass test rate (0% to 10%), contact trace and

28

test rate (0% to 50%), transmission rate (probability of transmission per contact per day), and

29

contact rate (number of contacts per person per day). We simulated four levels of transmission

30

rate, 14% (average baseline), 8% (average for face mask use), 5.4% (average for 3ft distancing),

31

and 2.5% (average for 6ft distancing and face mask use), interpolating results to the full range to

32

understand the impact of uncertainty in effectiveness, feasibility, and adherence of face mask use

33

and physical distancing. We evaluated contact rates between 1 and 25, to identify the threshold

34

that, if exceeded, could lead to several deaths.

35

When transmission rate was 8%, for trace and test at 50%, the contact rate threshold was 8.

36

However, any time delays in trace, test, and isolation quickly raised the number of deaths.

37

Keeping contact rate to 3 or below was more robust to testing delays, keeping deaths below 1 up

38

to a delay of 5 days from the time of infection to diagnosis and isolation. For a contact rate of 3,

39

the number of trace and tests peaked to about 70 per day and relaxed to 25 with the addition of

40

10% mass test. When transmission rate was 5.4%, for trace and test at 50%, the contact rate

41

threshold was 10. However, keeping contact rate to 4 or below was more robust to delays in

42

testing, keeping deaths below 1 up to a delay of 6 days from the time of infection to diagnosis

43

and isolation. For contact rate of 4, the number of trace and tests peaked at 50 per day and

44

relaxed to 10 per day with the addition of 10% mass test.

45

Threshold estimates can help develop on-campus scheduling and indoor-spacing plans in

46

conjunction with plans for asymptomatic testing for COVID-19. Combination thresholds should

47

be selected specific to the setting based on an assessment of the feasibility and resource

48

availability for testing and quarantine.

Introduction

50

The COVID-19 pandemic caused by the SARS-CoV-2 virus has caused significant disease and

51

economic burdens since its first outbreak in December 2019. In the absence of an effective

52

vaccine, the main intervention for the prevention of COVID-19 transmissions has been social

53

distancing. The most effective social distancing being lockdowns of non-essential organizations

54

and services, as adopted by several states since March, 2020, in efforts to immediately slow

55

down the pandemic [1]. However, lockdowns are a huge threat to the economic stability of a

56

nation as seen by the unprecedented rise in unemployment rates [2] [3]. Therefore, while

57

lockdowns are a good short-term strategy, for a long-term strategy or until a vaccine becomes

58

widely available, it has become necessary to identify alternate strategies and lifestyles that

59

control the disease burden while minimizing the economic burden. Interventions that are

60

effective include the use of face masks, physical distancing between persons at a recommended

61

6ft, and contact tracing and testing or mass testing to enable early diagnosis in the asymptomatic

62

stage of infection [4]. However, removal of lockdowns should be strictly accompanied by a

63

reopening plan that rapidly and efficiently enables the adoption of the above interventions, to

64

avoid an epidemic rebound. In addition to public health agencies, all members of a community,

65

in both public and private sectors, play a key role in the development and implementation of a

66

reopening plan that is most suited for their organization [5]. Among these sectors, universities

67

and colleges bear a special burden to develop a reopening plan that include changes to a range of

68

activities related to teaching, research, dining, housing, and extra-curricular activities [6].

Application to a university setting

313

Demographic data: We used the Fall 2018 student enrollment data from the University of

314

Massachusetts Amherst, Amherst, MA, to determine the population size of undergraduate and

315

graduate students and their age and gender distributions [44]. For faculty and staff, we used the

316

age distribution of persons 25 years and older from the Town of Amherst, MA, where the

317

university is located [45]. To initiate an outbreak, we assumed 4 to 5 infected cases on day 1

318

based on the following. We assumed that the proportion of incoming students who are infected

319

would be equal to the prevalence of Massachusetts. We also assumed that all incoming students

320

would be tested, and about 10% of infected cases would be false negatives. Prevalence is

321

unknown, as not all cases are diagnosed and diagnosed cases are not specifically tracked.

322

Therefore, to estimate prevalence of COVID-19 in MA, we used the simulation model to

323

determine the ratio of new diagnosis to persons with infection and applied that ratio to the

324

number of new diagnoses on June 26th in MA. This resulted in about 5 infected cases on day 1

325

remaining undetected, thus initiating an outbreak. We also assumed that at the beginning of

326

every week, there would be about 3 to 4 infections from outside, calculated by assuming that

327

about 10% of the population are likely to mix with the population outside the university or travel

328

out of Amherst during weekends and are not tested upon return. Based on the above, we

329

initialized the model on Day 1 with 4 to 5 infected persons in the Latent stage and added 3 to 4

330

outside cases to the Latent state at the beginning of every week. We simulated the model for a

331

90-day period to represent the duration of the expected Fall semester.

Discussions

433

This work estimates, under varying combinations of mass test, trace and test, and transmission

434

rate (physical distancing and face mask use), the contact rate thresholds, which if exceeded, lead

435

to rapid growth of the epidemic causing multiple deaths. It also estimates, under varying

436

combinations of mass test, trace and test, transmission rate, and contact rate, the number of trace

437

and tests, quarantines, hospitalizations, and deaths.

438
439

It is difficult to determine how the threshold contact rate estimates compare to those expected at

440

a university, as the data on contact rates prior to the COVID-19 outbreak are unavailable. One

441

study, conducted prior to the COVID-19 outbreak, estimated an average rate of 21 among

442

middle-aged adults in Portland, Oregon [46], however, contact rates in a university are likely to

443

be different. After the COVID-19 outbreak, more studies to estimate contact rates are emerging,

444

however, they are under the context of state-issued stay-at-home orders, and thus are

445

representative of the lower bounds. In one such poll [47] of U.S. adults, those who self-asses

20

(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted medRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity.
It is made available under a CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license .

446

their status as â€˜completely isolatedâ€™ (about 27%) reported a median (and mean) non-household

447

contact rate of 0 (and 1.5), those who self-asses their status as â€˜mostly isolatedâ€™ (about 47%),

448

reported a median (and mean) non-household contact rate of 2 (and 5.4), and those who self-

449

asses their status as â€˜partially isolatedâ€™ (about 17%), reported a median and mean non-household

450

contact rate of 2 (and 5.4). Among those listing their work sector as â€˜educationâ€™ (which included

451

library and training services), reported a median (and mean) work contact rate of 1 (and 2.6).

452

Another study [48] reported a median (and mean) contact rate of 2 (and 2.7) among a nationally

453

representative survey of the US population, with 85% of surveyors reporting four or fewer

454

contacts. The above studies were under maximum lockdowns, and thus, how these would change

455

upon reopening, and specifically at a university, are unknown, but can be expected to be higher

456

thus requiring careful control.

457
458

Another challenge in relating these threshold contact rates to that expected at a university arises

459

from the uncertainty in what defines a â€˜contactâ€™. In the above studies, the term contact rate has

460

varied definitions, such as the number of people who are < 6ft away from a person, the number

461

of people touched by another person, or the number of people with who a person has face to face

462

conversations. For epidemic spread, a contact is defined as any contact that exposes one to the

463

infection. Under the context of the SARS-CoV-2 virus, the above definitions are insufficient as

464

aerosol and environmental (surfaces) modes of transmission do not require one to be in direct

465

contact [49] [50] [51] [52]. On the other hand, use of face masks, physical distancing, and

466

sanitizing and cleaning, due to mandated policies or increased awareness, could reduce the

467

exposure from those in the vicinity [17] [19] [20] [21] [22]. Current definitions for contact

468

combine the two.

Given the above challenges, in this study we attempted to separate out contact rates from

470

transmission rates. Considering the uncertainty in the baseline transmission rate, and in the

471

expected reductions from face mask use and physical distancing [12], uncertainties rising from

472

inherent features, feasibility, and compliance, we interpolated results for the full range of

473

transmission rate. The transmission rates simulated here cover the range of estimates from the

474

literature for baseline 14% (11.6%-17%), and expected reductions from use of surgical or cloth

475

face masks in non-health care settings 7.8% (5.6%-11.1%), 3ft physical distancing 6.9% (3.6%-

476

13%), and 6ft physical distancing 2.8%(1.4%-5.7%), calculated using relative risks from a meta-

477

analysis study that, in addition to SARS-CoV-2, evaluated viruses of similarly high virulence

478

[12] (see Supplemental Appendix Table S2).

479
480

The contact rate thresholds in this study can help inform decisions related to planning of indoor

481

spacing and personnel scheduling, by eliminating scenarios that generate contact rates that cross

482

the threshold. However, the selection of contact rates should be considered in conjunction with

483

testing rates and transmission rates. The contact rate thresholds were low when trace and test

484

rates were low or transmission rates were higher than 5.4%. Though the contact rate thresholds

485

under low transmission rates and 50% trace and test were higher, going above 5, the feasibility of

486

finding, testing, and isolating infected contacts within 2 days of infection (50% trace and test)

487

should be evaluated specific to the setting of implementation. When transmission rate was 5.4%

488

or below and contact rate was higher than 5, any delays in trace and test generated rapid

489

accumulation of the number of persons to trace and test with the peak numbers per day going

490

above 50, and eventually leading to several deaths if testing delays continued. When

491

transmission rate was 5.4%, a contact rate threshold of 4 was more robust to testing delays, and

when transmission rate was 8%, a contact rate threshold of 3 was more robust to these testing

493

delays.

495

In addition to contact rate thresholds, the resource availabilities for testing and quarantining,

496

should be considered in selection of a strategy. Addition of mass test to trace and test generally

497

had a significant reduction in the peak number of trace and tests. In comparing between choice of

498

mass tests or trace and tests, the trade-offs are between high false positives requiring more

499

resources for quarantining in mass test versus low quarantine resource needs but high tracing

500

resource needs for trace and test. Availability of resources for trace and test versus quarantine

501

should be evaluated specific to the setting.

502
503

Our work is subject to limitations. Our model is deterministic. We did not specifically model

504

false positives hence, the estimates here only provide a lower bound. We used an average contact

505

rate for all persons, in order to help decisions related to designing a controlled environment, such

506

as controlling workplace scheduling and layouts and issuing uniform guidelines. We did not

507

model contact rates to be representative of actual expected behaviors of individuals or to be

508

representative of actual expected networks between individuals. We did not explicitly model

509

other interventions that could reduce transmission rate such as controlled ventilation, filtering air

510

and controlling air flow, which are likely to impact transmissions [53]. The transmission rates

511

evaluated should be used with caution. The baseline estimate of 14% is an average estimate, and

512

the estimates for face masks and physical distancing are relative to these estimates. For a

513

different baseline transmission rate, the interpolated values of transmission rates should be used

514

to determine expected reduction. We did not model other flu like illnesses and thus we did not

(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted medRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity.

515

assess the additional healthcare resource needs such as testing and quarantining because of

516

similarity in symptoms with COVID-19.

517
518

Despite these limitations, the results from this work could collectively help inform development

519

of a preparedness plan for reopening of a University. Contact rates can help plan indoor spacing

520

and personnel scheduling of classrooms, labs and office spaces, housing, and dining. The

521

estimates for the number of tests and quarantines, presented here under each scenario, can help

522

determine the resource needs such as personnel, equipment, and infrastructure. Though off-

523

campus contact rates cannot be controlled, estimates of threshold contact rates can help eliminate

524

indoor spacing and personnel scheduling scenarios that exceed these contact rate thresholds that

525

have a high chance of an epidemic. The threshold contact rates can only help in elimination of

526

strategies and should not be used as a metric for selection of a strategy. The uncertainty ranges in

527

results suggest that selection of a strategy should collectively consider indoor spacing, personnel

528

scheduling, testing, and quarantining. Selection of a scenario should be done after a feasibility

529

assessment that compares resource needs under each scenario to excepted resource availability

530

for testing and quarantining, and risk assessment to determine the ability to control transmission

531

rate through use of face masks and physical distancing, including its feasibility and compliance.
