In the first quarter of 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic brought the world to a

6

state of paralysis. During this period, humanity has seen by far the largest

7

organized travel restrictions and unprecedented efforts and global coordina-

8

tion to contain the spread of the SARS-CoV-2 virus. Using large scale human

9

mobility and fine grained epidemic incidence data, we develop a framework

10

to understand and quantify the effectiveness of the interventions implemented

11

by various countries to control epidemic growth. Our analysis reveals the im-

12

portance of timing and implementation of strategic policy in controlling the

13

epidemic. Through our analysis, we also unearth significant spatial diffusion

14

of the epidemic before and during the lock-down measures in several coun-

15

tries, casting doubt on the effectiveness or on the implementation quality of

16

the proposed Governmental policies.

1 Introduction

18

The pandemic due to the SARS-CoV-2 virus (1) has been impacting the world population, health

19

care system and economies over the first half of 2020 (2). Since its identification in December

20

2019 in Wuhan, China, this novel coronavirus disease (COVID-19) has been spreading in China

21

in Jan.-Feb. 2020. The epidemic development was detected in Italy in the second half of

22

February and progressively diffused in the rest of the world. It was declared a global pandemic

23

on March 11, 2020 by the World Health Organization (WHO) (3). We have all witnessed and

24

experienced a series of interventions with various levels of confinement measures in different

25

countries and regions, aimed at decreasing the effective reproduction number Rt and controlling

26

the epidemics.

27

Our starting hypothesis is that the development of infected cases, of the various forms of

28

illnesses resulting from infection and of the death rates reflect the interplay between the biolog-

29

ical and epidemiological properties of this new SARS-CoV-2 virus and the political, cultural,

30

sociological and governance characteristics of different nations and human communities. By

31

combining mobility data, epidemiological data and clinical data across ten countries, we de-

32

velop a modelling framework to quantify the effectiveness of the interventions implemented by

33

various countries to control the epidemic growth. This shock provides a real-life natural exper-

34

iment to falsify the effectiveness of different organisations and interventions and policies, with

35

the goal of informing and guiding future plans against potential second and third waves of the

36

epidemics as well as future outbreaks in general.

37

First, we quantify precisely how interventions in the form of mobility restrictions and social

38

distancing have had significant impacts in controlling the development of the epidemics in many

39

countries, as measured by the decrease in reproduction number Rt and the extend of curbing the

40

increase of infected cases. We also document a surprisingly large heterogeneity in the reduction

41

of Rt across regions within a given country and also across countries. Further, we observe non

42

monotonous and fluctuating time dependence of Rt in different regions.

43

Our most surprising result is that, in many regions where the epidemic was not visible, the

44

interventions led to a significant transient increase in reproduction number Rt , in contradiction

45

with the short-term objective of lockdown measures. For instance, in several regions of France

52

We also quantify how the timing of lockdown determined the trajectory of the epidemic

53

54

55

the lockdown date and was developing silently as revealed by the strong spatial diffusion of

56

the epidemic after the lockdown. In other countries, the epidemic was better contained by

57

intervention measures.

58

Our analysis overall suggests that the interventions may have not been optimal and that there

59

are probably better alternatives to complete lockdowns. Our study reveals the importance of

60

timing and targeting of interventions as a likely better strategy compared with undifferentiated

61

lockdown. We observe belated intervention at the regional and local levels and hasty global

62

lockdowns, which, in a number of regions, result in disappointing reduction of the reproduction

63

number and curbing of the increase of new cases compared to other comparable countries.

3 Discussion

156

We have found important to quantify the incubation and confirmation periods in order to assess

157

the impact of policy interventions to contain the epidemics in different regions. This quantifi-

158

cation shown in figures 10 and S1 allows us to define a credible time interval over which to

159

quantify the impact of interventions. With this, our analysis provides a framework to under6

stand the effectiveness of the policies and interventions implemented by various countries to

161

control the epidemic growth. The wealth of results presented in figures 1-9 leads to the follow-

162

ing insights.

163

Overall, it is clear, and unsurprising, that interventions in the form of mobility restrictions

164

and social distancing are found to have significant impacts in controlling the development of

165

the epidemics in many countries. In particular, we have quantified the decrease in reproduction

166

number Rt resulting from the intervention. As an illustration shown in fig. 1, for the Hessen

167

state in Germany, we are able to quantify that intervention in this state reduced Rt by about 1

168

unit compared with the counterfactual scenario of no intervention.

169

More surprising is the large heterogeneity in the reduction of Rt across different states in

170

Germany, as shown in figure 2. For this country, intervention measures had led systematically

171

to a decrease in Rt , with quite strong differences from state to state. Even more surprising is the

172

time dependence which exhibits a non monotonous and fluctuating behaviour of Rt in different

173

regions.

174

For most of regions in different countries, because of the interventions, Rt decreased, how-

175

ever there are some places where it increased. Because this increase is transient and constrained

176

by the lockdown, it does not lead to a very strong explosion of new cases. A tentative interpre-

177

tation is that, as lockdown was considered and being implemented, in a number of regions, it

178

triggered large movements of people to relocate, hence promoting new contagions and epicen-

179

ters for the epidemics to mature after the lockdown. An additional mechanism, which underlies

180

the Japanese policy, is the effect of closed spaces and close-contact settings within confined

181

households, which has been shown to lead to increased infections within households for in-

182

stance. But because the lockdown only allows the new nucleii of contagion to develop locally,

183

the number of cases did not explode. Examples of this effect can be found in Luzern and

184

Solothurn of Switzerland, in Bremen of Germany, in Saga Ken of Japan and in Odisha of India.

185

This effect that has previously been described qualitatively is given here a quantitative support

186

by our systematic analysis.

187

The observation that lockdown led first to an increase in Rt in a significant number of regions

188

and countries is confirmed by the correlation analysis presented in fig. 9 relating increase of

189

cases to changes of Rt in different countries. There are many regions in each country where
