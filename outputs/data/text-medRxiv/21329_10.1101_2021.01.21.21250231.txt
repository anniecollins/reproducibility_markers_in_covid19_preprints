Abstract

33

The COVID-19 disease, caused by the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus 2

34

(SARS-CoV-2), is a very contagious disease that has killed many people around the world.

35

According to the World Health Organization (WHO) data, the spread of the disease appears to be

36

slower in Africa. Although a number of studies have been published on the relationship between

37

meteorological parameters and COVID-19 transmission, the effects of climate conditions on

38

COVID-19 remain largely unexplored and without consensus following the main research finding

39

over Africa (often based on a single country or city). Here, using available epidemiological data

40

over 275 days (i.e., from March 1 to November 30, 2020) taken from the European Center for

41

Disease Prevention and Control of the European Union database and daily data of surface air

42

temperature and humidity from the National Center for Environmental Prediction (NCEP), this

43

paper investigates the potential contributions of climate conditions on COVID-19 transmission over

44

16 countries selected from three bioclimatic regions of Africa (i.e., Sahel, Maghreb and Gulf of

45

Guinea). On average, our main findings highlight statistically significant inverse correlations

46

between COVID-19 cases and temperature over the Maghreb and the Gulf of Guinea regions,

47

whereas positive correlations are found in the Sahel, especially over the central part including Niger

48

and Mali. Correlations with specific humidity and water vapor parameters display significant and

49

positive values over the Sahelian and the Gulf of Guinean countries and negative values over the

50

Maghreb countries. In other word, results imply that the COVID-19 pandemic transmission is

51

influenced differently across the three bioclimatic regions: i) cold and dry environmental conditions

52

over the Maghreb; ii) warm and humid conditions over the Sahel iii) cold and humid conditions

53

over the Gulf of Guinea. These findings could be useful for decision-makers who plan public health

54

and control measures in affected African countries and would have substantial implications for

55

directing respiratory disease surveillance activities.

Introduction

67

The coronaviruses constitute a family of various viruses affecting both humans and animals.

68

In late December 2019, the World Health Organization (WHO) was informed about an epidemic of

69

"pneumonia of unknown cause" detected in the city of Wuhan (Hubei Province, China), the

70

seventh-largest city in China, with 11 million inhabitants [1,2]. The first reported infected

71

individuals were associated to the Wuhan seafood market in southern China. The virus causing the

72

epidemic was quickly determined to be a novel coronavirus linked to the Middle Eastern

73

Respiratory Syndrome Coronavirus (MERS-CoV) and to the Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome

74

Coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) [3]. Infected travelers, mainly by aircraft, are known to be

75

responsible for the introduction of the virus outside Wuhan [4]. The novel coronavirus so-called

76

2019-nCoV in the early parts of the epidemic has spread rapidly to multiple countries and has been

77

declared on March 11, 2020, as a pandemic by the WHO. The 2019-nCoV terminology changed

78

later and become Coronavirus Disease 2019 (COVID-19).

79

Researchers who have been working on this epidemic believe that this coronavirus probably

80

originates from bats [5,6]. This hypothesis is based on the SARS epidemic in 2003 that emerged in

81

southern China after being transmitted from the bat, its "natural reservoir", to humans [7]. Indeed,

82

the COVID-19 disease shares 80% of genetic similarity [7]. The infection caused a wide range of

83

symptoms and showed different degrees of severity with different fatality rates that are country

84

dependent. Symptoms may appear 2-14 days after exposure to the virus [8]. People with these

85

symptoms or combinations of symptoms may have cough and shortness of breath or difficulty

86

breathing. Transmission can take place by air, in contact with secretions or contaminated objects.

87

The mortality rate is found to be relatively lower compared to SARS-Cov which was estimated

88

around 15% [8]. The most sensitive people are the elderly, those suffering from respiratory and

89

immunosuppressed pathologies.

90
91

One of the central questions facing the scientific community is how climate conditions

92

contribute to COVID-19 transmission. Chan et al.,[9] investigations on the past SARS coronavirus

93

outbreaks have put in evidence that the viability of the virus was lost at high temperatures (e.g.,

94

38Â°C) and high humidity (e.g., > 95%) and this explains the lower number of cases in Asian

95

countries including Malaysia due to higher temperature and humidity. The first studies from China

96

have shown that the contagiousness of the COVID-19 is higher in the cities in the north with a

97

lower humidity value and lower temperatures than those over the south-eastern coasts with warm

98

and humid conditions [10,11]. Meteorological variables such as air temperature, humidity, and other

99

parameters including solar radiation have been also found to act differently concerning coronavirus

100

survival [12]. Although several studies have supported the hypothesis that climate conditions may

101

affect the COVID-19 pandemic transmission as happens with other viruses such as influenza

102

[13,14], respiratory syncytial virus [15,16], considerable disagreement is evident among the

103

different investigations (often based on different country or city); most of them have concluded that

104

the COVID-19 transmission prefers cold and dry weather conditions - i.e. temperature and humidity

105

related variables are negatively correlated to the virus transmission [12,17â€“22] and [23] (for a

106

review) -, while others studies have reported contradictory results showing that temperature and/or

107

humidity variables may positively associated, or not be associated with the COVID-19 expansion

108

[24â€“28]. The majority of the above-mentioned papers were focused on Asian and European

109

countries (i.e., mid-latitude regions). Few studies have investigated the effects of weather conditions

110

on the incidence of COVID-19 pandemic transmission over Africa and the results have even less

111

consensus (often based on a single country or city) [29â€“31].

112

In addition, it is worth pointing out that since the first cases of coronavirus appeared in Africa

113

(early March 2020), the spread of the disease seems to be progressing more slowly there than else-

114

where. The five most affected countries at this time are South Africa, Algeria, Morocco, Egypt, and

115

Cameroon, with a very slow infection rate compared to the mid-latitudes or south America countries

116

(see, Fig 1). Nevertheless, it is still not fully understood how climate conditions contribute to

117

COVID-19 transmission in Africa.

118

The outlined scenario shows that further studies and reviews are needed to extend the analysis

119

available in literature and to quantify the possible relationship between climate conditions and

120

coronavirus transmission in Africa. To address these issues, here, using available epidemiological

121

data over 275 days (1 March - November 30, 2020) extracted from the European Center for Disease

122

Prevention and Control of the European Union (https://www.ecdc.europa.eu/en/publications-

123

data/download-todays-data-geographic-distribution-covid-19-cases-worldwide) and daily data of

124

surface air temperature and humidity from the National Center for Environmental Prediction

125

(NCEP), the present study aimed to investigate the potential contributions of climate conditions on

126

COVID-19 pandemic transmission in 16 highly populated West and North African countries divided

127

into three bioclimatic regions (i.e., Maghreb, Sahel and Gulf of Guinea) [32].

128

The present work is organized as follows: Section 2 outlines the data used and the statistical

129

methods applied. Section 3 investigates the spatial distribution of COVID-19 confirmed cases

130

throughout countries in North and West Africa and their potential relationship with climate condi-

131

tions. Finally, a summary and discussion of the main findings are provided in Section 4.

Surveillance COVID-19 data

134

The clinical data corresponds to the number of COVID-19 cases over 16 West and North

138

139

142

143

November 30, 2020 and datasets are obtained at country level. These cases are clinically confirmed

144

by the Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) tests, as recommended by the WHO.

148

149

150

2.5Â°Ã— 2.5Â° horizontal resolution over the North and West Africa. Besides those variables, the Water

296

297

COVID-19 pandemic transmission in Africa were investigated and correlation coefficient values are

298

estimated using the robust and non-parametric Kendall (Ï„) (Fig 7) and Spearman (S1 Fig in

supplementary material) rank tests. In Fig 7 (and in S1 Fig), the statistically significant correlations

300

with 99% confidence intervals, that is, correlations that passed the Kendall/ Spearman rank tests at

0.01 level (i.e., p < 0.01; 99% C.I), are reported in green dots and those that passed the

302

303

305

306

consideration the incubation period. Note, we have chosen to show the correlation values calculated

307

Conclusions

357

An extension of the analysis available in literature for the quantification of the relationship between

358

climatic variables and COVID-19 pandemic transmission in North and West Africa is presented in

359

this paper. The contributions of climate conditions on the COVID-19 transmission have been

360

studied in 16 most highly populated West and North African countries divided into three bioclimatic

361

regions (i.e., the Maghreb, the Sahel and the Gulf of Guinea) based on KÃ¶ppen bioclimatic

364

365

[29â€“31], here, we considered as a reliable variable the residuals of the daily new positive cases with

366

respect to the robust and non-parametric Median-Based Linear Model (MBLM) defined in [49].

367

Further, when working on the residuals, makes our analysis independent on the analyzed time

368

period. Also, we have taken into consideration the incubation period, i.e., the correlations were

369

investigated against the residual of COVID-19 cases with a time-shift of 15 days, - i.e.,

370

meteorological data from 15 February 2020 to 15 November 2020 was used in the analysis. The

371

strongest finding of this paper is that the COVID-19 pandemic transmission is differently affected

372

across the three bioclimatic regions. First of all, over the Maghreb countries, our results highlight

373

that the COVID-19 transmission is strongly enhanced by a decrease of temperature associated with

374

375

376

377

378

379

380

381

382

383

384

385

386

the COVID-19 transmission. In other words, COVID-19 transmission is strongly enhanced by a

387

decrease of temperature associated with an increase of humidity and water vapor. Such findings also

388

confirm the previous findings available in the literature (e.g. [25,26]) and may related to the

389

observed peak of number cases experienced in most of the Gulf of Guinea countries during the

390

period May-August (Fig 4C and Fig 6E and S2C Fig).

394

395

COVID-19 cases with a time-shift of 15 days, i.e., taking into consideration the incubation period.
