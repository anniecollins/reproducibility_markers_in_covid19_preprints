Abstract
The reproductive number â„›0 (and its value after initial disease emergence â„›) has long been used to
predict the likelihood of pathogen invasion, to gauge the potential severity of an epidemic, and to set
policy around interventions. However, often ignored complexities have generated confusion around use
of the metric. This is particularly apparent with the emergent pandemic virus SARS-CoV-2, the causative
agent of COVID-19. We address some of these misconceptions, namely, how â„› changes over time,
varies over space, and relates to epidemic size by referencing the mathematical definition of â„› and
examples from the current pandemic. We hope that a better appreciation of the uses, nuances, and
limitations of â„› facilitates a better understanding of epidemic spread, epidemic severity, and the effects
of interventions in the context of SARS-CoV-2.

Introduction
With the emergence of SARS-CoV-2, the novel coronavirus responsible for COVID-19, much attention
has been given to the reproductive number, â„›, and its initial state, â„›0 (Viceconte and Petrosillo, 2020).
â„›0 is the expected number of infections generated by an infected individual in an otherwise fully
susceptible population (Anderson and May, 1991; Diekmann et al., 1990). Under relatively general
assumptions, â„›0 can be used to determine the probability an emerging disease will cause an epidemic,
the final size of an epidemic, and what level of vaccination would be required to achieve herd immunity
(Anderson and May, 1991; Delamater et al., 2019; Heffernan et al., 2005; Roberts, 2007). Therefore,
when interpreted correctly, and in conjunction with additional relevant information, it can yield valuable
insight. However, misinterpretation may lead to faulty conclusions regarding disease dynamics.
The virus, SARS-CoV-2 emerged in Wuhan, China in late 2019 and has since become pandemic
causing over 413,000 deaths worldwide by June 10th, 2020 (â€œJohns Hopkins University & Medicine
Coronavirus Resource Center,â€ 2020) in addition to severe economic distress. Policy makers have relied

Misconception 1: â„›0 explains future dynamics
As we have explained, â„›0 is calculated during the early stages of an epidemic because of its value in
determining future infection dynamics (Anderson and May, 1991; Ma and Earn, 2006). But â„› changes
over time in two important ways that limit its value in understanding future dynamics: first as awareness
of infection leads hosts to alter their behavior, and second, as outbreaks progress and new hosts
become limiting.
Shifts in behavior that influence contact rates ğ‘˜ or the probability of infection given contact ğ‘
can alter the reproductive number â„› over extremely short timescales. For example, as awareness of the
SARS-CoV-2 epidemic grew in the United States in March 2020, human mobility ground to a near halt
(Gao et al., 2020; Warren and Skillman, 2020), presumably reducing contact rates ğ‘˜. Other individual
behavioral changes such as increased handwashing and mask wearing (Belot et al., 2020; Goldberg et al.,
2020) have likely reduced the probability of transmission given contact ğ‘ (Liang et al., 2020). Such
bottom-up forces combined with top-down government-imposed interventions (e.g. school closures,
banned gatherings) reduced â„› to below 1 (the threshold for epidemic persistence) by late April in some
states (Johndrow et al., 2020; Miller et al., 2020). Similar reductions to â„› were documented in China (R.

behavioral changes may cause â„› to fluctuate above and below 1 at different times based on the
perceived threat of COVID-19. If that is the case, behavioral fluctuations and intermittent lockdowns

While behavioral changes can temporarily reduce â„› as described above, more sustainable

through naturally acquired immunity or vaccination. However, the impact of removing susceptible
individuals is often more complicated than under assumptions of classical SIR models such as eq. 2.12.3. When transmission rates are heterogeneous within a population, meaning that some individuals are
more likely to contract infection than others, â„› declines faster than predicted by eq. 2.1-2.3 (May and

Misconception 2: The reproductive number is constant over space
The â„›0 of many pathogens are often referred to as known values. For example, the â„›0 of measles is 1214, polio is 5-7, and pertussis is 12-17 (Doherty et al., 2016). For SARS-CoV-2, estimates typically range
from 2-3 (Ying Liu et al., 2020). However, the parameters (ğ‘˜, ğ‘, and ğ‘ƒ) that make up â„›0 can differ
substantially from place to place (Figure 1, Delamater et al., 2019). It follows that interventions to
reduce â„› to less than 1 may need to vary in aggressiveness across locations (Stier et al. 2020).
Since â„›0 differs between groups of people, combining multiple groups together to estimate a
population-wide â„›0 can produce misleading notions of disease spread. For example, though high
measles vaccination rates in the United States keep â„› below 1 nation-wide, smaller unvaccinated
communities still experience serious outbreaks (Leslie et al., 2018). In the current COVID-19 pandemic,
disease transmission has so far been much higher in refugee and low income populations compared to
non-refugee and high income populations (Chopra and Sobel, 2020; Lau et al., 2020; Ruiz-Euler et al.,
2020). Since â„›0 is an average, combining communities with high and low transmission may yield an
estimate of â„›0 <1, yet disease may still readily spread (Li et al., 2011). On the other hand, splitting
populations may mean missing transmission events that occur between populations, thus
underestimating â„›0 (Smith et al., 2009).

Conclusions
As we have discussed, the reproductive number â„› and its initial value â„›0 can be used to assess the
potential for disease invasion and persistence, to predict the extent of an epidemic, and to infer the
impact of interventions and of relaxing control measures. However, the utility of â„› and â„›0 can easily be
overstated. We have focused on three misconceptions that can lead to inaccurate perceptions of
disease dynamics. These misconceptions are problematic no matter the complexity of the model or the
reliability of the data used to estimate â„› because populations vary over space and time and in their
changing responses to disease. Considering these nuances when interpreting â„› allows for a stronger

understanding of the patterns with which SARS-CoV-2 virus has traversed the globe, why it has impacted
some populations more than others, and how best to limit future transmission.
