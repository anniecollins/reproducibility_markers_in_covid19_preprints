ABSTRACT

21

Heat treatment denatures viral proteins that comprise the virion, making virus incapable of

22

infecting a host. Coronavirus (CoV) virions contain single-stranded RNA genomes with a lipid

23

envelope and 4 proteins, 3 of which are associated with the lipid envelope and thus are thought to

24

be easily denatured by heat or surfactant-type chemicals. Prior studies have shown that a

25

temperature of as low as 75 ‚ÑÉ and treatment duration of 15 min can effectively inactivate CoV.

26

The applicability of a CoV heat inactivation method greatly depends on the length of time of a

27

heat treatment and the temperature needed to inactivate the virus. With the goal of finding

28

conditions where sub-second heat exposure of CoV can sufficiently inactivate CoV, we designed

29

and developed a simple system that can measure sub-second heat inactivation of CoV. The

30

system is composed of capillary stainless-steel tubing immersed in a temperature-controlled oil

31

bath followed by an ice bath, through which virus solution can be flowed at various speeds.

32

Flowing virus solution at different speeds, along with a real-time temperature monitoring system,

33

allows the virus to be accurately exposed to a desired temperature for various durations of time.

34

Using mouse hepatitis virus (MHV), a beta-coronavirus, as a model system, we identified that

35

85.2 ‚ÑÉ for 0.48 s exposure is sufficient to obtain > 5 Log10 reduction in viral titer (starting titer:

36

5 √ó 107 PFU/mL), and that when exposed to 83.4 ‚ÑÉ for 0.95 s, the virus was completely

37

inactivated (zero titer, > 6 Log10 reduction).

38

IMPORTANCE

39

Three coronaviruses (CoVs) have now caused global outbreaks within the past 20 years, with the

40

COVID19 pandemic caused by SARS-CoV-2 still ongoing. Methods that can rapidly inactivate

41

viruses, especially CoVs, can play critical roles in ensuring public safety and safeguarding

42

personal health. Heat treatment of viruses to inactive them can be an efficient and inexpensive

method, with the potential to be incorporated into various human-occupied spaces. In this work,

1 Introduction

55

Severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) is the virus responsible for the

56

currently ongoing global pandemic, the coronavirus disease of 2019 (COVID-19) (1-3). The

57

main transmission routes of SARS-CoV-2 include direct or indirect contact with objects or

58

contaminated surfaces, short-range person-to-person transmission via droplets from coughing or

59

sneezing, and long-range airborne transmission via aerosols (4). Therefore, environmental

60

sterilization and virus inactivation are of great importance to prevent and control the spread of

61

the virus. Currently, the most commonly used methods to sterilize or inactivate viruses include

62

treatment using chemical agents, UV irradiation exposure, and heat treatment, which have all

63

been intensively assessed and reported (5-10). Compared with other methods, one major

64

advantage of heat treatment is its relatively shorter treatment time and simplistic method, along

65

with the ability to be incorporated into various human-occupied space (11-13), which allows for

66

the technique to be readily implemented into a variety of existing applications or systems that

67

could be readily retrofitted to add rapid pathogen inactivation functionality, such as existing

68

heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems as well as sewer systems.

69

Heat inactivation is a relatively easy, safe, and efficient method to disinfect coronavirus (CoV),

70

as CoV is an enveloped virus that is surrounded by a lipid bilayer with viral spike proteins

71

projecting from the lipid envelope, where both the envelope and the spike protein are susceptible

72

to heat (14). Previous studies have shown that at a temperature of 56 ‚ÑÉ and higher, with heat

73

application time typically longer than 1 min, is needed to efficiently inactivate CoVs such as

74

SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV (> 6 Log10 reduction) (5, 6, 13). More specifically, at relatively low

75

treatment temperatures (56 - 65 ‚ÑÉ), treatment time of 15 - 60 min was required, while at higher

76

treatment temperatures (70 - 100 ‚ÑÉ) a much shorter duration of 1 to 15 min was needed (5, 6, 13,

1

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.10.05.327528; this version posted October 6, 2020. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

77

15-17). For example, heat treatment of SARS-CoV-2 at 70 ‚ÑÉ for 5 min achieved > 4.5 Log10

78

reduction (15), with another study reported that heat treatment at 92 ‚ÑÉ for 15 min achieved > 6

79

Log10 reduction for SARS-CoV-2 (5). However, for heat treatment to be effectively utilized for

80

liquid and airborne CoV inactivation in broad ranges of practical settings, such methods need to

81

be effective at a significantly shorter heat treatment time (even if the temperature itself has to be

82

higher), otherwise there is limited practicality in such heat treatment methods. For example,

83

having to increase the temperature of liquid for minutes would consume large amount of energy,

84

and having to treat air for minutes becomes impractical.

85

Here, we hypothesize that a much shorter heat treatment time may be sufficient to destroy key

86

components of CoVs (e.g., envelope or the spike proteins) to completely inactivate CoV.

87

Conventional heat treatment testing method mostly utilizes a simple method of dipping a CoV-

88

containing tube into a temperature-controlled water bath. Such methods are valid when heat

89

treatment time in the range of minutes are utilized, but cannot be used if seconds or sub-second

90

temperature exposure needs to be tested. In this study, we developed a simple flow-through

91

heating and cooling method utilizing a stainless steel capillary tube, and used the method to

92

investigate the effect of CoV heat treatment at an extremely short heat exposure time of 0.1 - 1 s

93

at an actually applied temperature range of 35-100 ‚ÑÉ. This study provides essential data for the

94

development of sub-second CoV heat inactivation approaches, including methods to efficiently

95

inactivate airborne CoVs indoors. As all CoVs are surrounded by lipid bilayer membranes with

96

similar proteins and have similar physical properties (14), we expect that our finding using

97

mouse hepatitis virus (MHV), a beta-coronavirus, as a model system, can be broadly applicable

98

to CoVs in general, including SARS-CoV, SARS-CoV-2, and MERS-CoV, to name a few. Since

99

many other viruses, such as dengue virus, influenza virus, and measles virus, are also enveloped

2

100

viruses where the envelopes contain surface proteins (18), we expect that this heat inactivation

101

method can have broad utility for many other viruses of global consequences.

3 Results and Discussion

191

3.1 Validation of the viral solution treatment temperature

192

As our study aims to inactivate CoVs with a sub-second exposure to high temperature, accurately

193

measuring the real temperature applied to the viral solution is imperative. Since the solution

194

cannot be heated up or cooled down instantaneously and the desired temperature exposure time

195

is quite short, this is non-trivial. First, to validate whether the thermocouple inserted into the T-

196

junction of the SS tube (called T-thermocouple here) can accurately measure the solution

197

temperature inside the SS tube, a solution with known temperature, set by heating the bulk of the

198

solution in a temperature-controlled water bath, was drawn into the SS tubing at various flow

199

speeds and its temperature was measured. This measured temperature inside the SS tubing (2 cm

200

away from the heated water bath) was then compared to that of the bulk solution temperature.

201

Fig. S1-B in the ESI shows these calibration curves. The results show that the temperature probe

202

accurately measures the solution temperature inside the SS tube. For example, at a fast flow of

203

0.1 s exposure, which minimizes any cooling of the solution outside of the water bath, the
7

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/2020.10.05.327528; this version posted October 6, 2020. The copyright holder for this preprint
(which was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

204

measured temperature was almost identical to the water bath temperature (Fig. S1-B, 0.1 s

205

condition, 97.8 ‚ÑÉ real temperature = 97.7 ‚ÑÉ measured temperature). Second, as the flow rate

206

293

a system and protocol that can inactivate infectibility of MHV. We successfully developed an

294

experimental setup that allows applying sub-second duration of heat to the CoV solution where
