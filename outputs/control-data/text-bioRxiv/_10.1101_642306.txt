Abstract

36

Understanding the genomic signatures, genes, and traits underlying local adaptation of organisms

37

to heterogeneous environments is of central importance to the field evolutionary biology. Mixed

38

linear mrsodels that identify allele associations to environment, while controlling for genome-

39

wide variation at other loci, have emerged as the method of choice when studying local

40

adaptation. Despite their importance, it is unclear whether this approach performs better than

41

identifying environmentally-associated SNPs without accounting for population structure. To

42

examine this, we first use the mixed linear model GEMMA, and simple Spearman correlations,

43

to identify SNPs showing significant associations to climate with and without accounting for

44

population structure. Subsequently, using Italy and Sweden populations, we compare evidence of

45

allele frequency differentiation (F ST ), linkage disequilibrium (LD), fitness variation, and

46

functional constraint, underlying these SNPs. Using a lenient cut-off for significance, we find

47

that SNPs identified by both approaches, and SNPs uniquely identified by Spearman

48

correlations, were enriched at sites showing genomic evidence of local adaptation and function

49

but were limited across Quantitative Trait Loci (QTL) explaining fitness variation. SNPs

50

uniquely identified by GEMMA, showed no direct or indirect evidence of local adaptation, and

51

no enrichment along putative functional sites. Finally, SNPs that showed significantly high F ST

52

and LD, were enriched along fitness QTL peaks and cis-regulatory/nonsynonymous sites

53

showing significant functional constraint. Using these SNPs, we identify genes underlying

54

fitness QTL, and genes linking flowering time to local adaptation. These include a regulator of

55

abscisic-acid (FLDH) and flowering time genes PIF3, FIO1, and COL5.

Introduction

61

Populations of a species may inhabit different environments where local selection pressures

62

favor a combination of (multivariate) phenotypes (Leimu and Fischer 2008; Conover, et al. 2009;

63

Hereford 2009; Savolainen, et al. 2013). Once locally adapted, the resident genotype is expected,

64

on average, to have a higher relative fitness than a foreign genotype (Kawecki and Ebert 2004).

65

Despite the widespread evidence of local adaptation in many taxa (Leimu and Fischer 2008;

66

Jeong and Di Rienzo 2014; Arguello, et al. 2016), our understanding of the traits involved, its

67

genetic basis, and its environmental underpinnings is still at an infant stage (Savolainen, et al.

68

2013; Tiffin and Ross-Ibarra 2014; Wadgymar, et al. 2017).

69
70

In a variety of species, reciprocal transplant and common garden/laboratory experiments have

71

showed significant adaptive differentiation between natural populations inhabiting different

72

environments (Via 1991; Hendry, et al. 2002; Savolainen, et al. 2007; Ã…gren and Schemske

73

2012; Kaufmann, et al. 2017; Phifer-Rixey, et al. 2018). Furthermore, in plants and animals,

74

mapping experiments have uncovered Quantitative Trait Loci (QTL) for traits that are thought to

75

underlie local adaptation (Colosimo, et al. 2004; Oakley, et al. 2014; Yang, et al. 2016; Ã…gren, et

76

al. 2017), in addition to QTL explaining fitness differences across environments (Ã…gren, et al.

77

2013; Anderson, et al. 2013). Despite the importance of QTL studies in providing direct

78

evidence for local adaptation (Ã…gren, et al. 2013), in many instances they provide a low

79

resolution for its genetic basis, and in practical terms are time consuming, expensive, and labor

80

intense (Joosen, et al. 2009).

With the advent of low-cost, and fast, next generation sequencing (Henson, et al. 2012), higher

83

resolution population genomics approaches have emerged as the new means for examining the

84

genetic basis of local adaptation. (Lachance and Tishkoff 2013; Savolainen, et al. 2013; Tiffin

85

and Ross-Ibarra 2014; Sork 2017). In brief, these methods include: (a) identifying single

86

nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) showing significant allele frequency differentiation between

87

populations (F ST ) (Beaumont and Balding 2004; Foll and Gaggiotti 2008; de Villemereuil and

88

Gaggiotti 2015); (b) identifying genomic regions showing significant increases in linkage

89

disequilibrium (Jacobs, et al. 2016) or composite likelihood ratios for recent sweeps (DeGiorgio,

90

et al. 2016; Huber, et al. 2016); and (c) alleles showing significant correlations to

91

environment/climate (Hancock, et al. 2011; Jones, et al. 2012; Lasky, et al. 2012; Lasky, et al.

92

2014; Pluess, et al. 2016; Yeaman, et al. 2016; Monroe, et al. 2018; Price, et al. 2018). The latter

93

approach has gained particular attention because it can be implemented on the basis of individual

94

(as opposed to population-based) sampling, and furthermore it provides a direct link to

95

ecologically relevant factors (e.g., climate).

96
97

Despite the ability of population genomic methods to identify candidate genetic variation

98

underlying local adaptation, it is hard to disentangle the effects of selection from demographic

99

history (Lotterhos and Whitlock 2014; Hoban, et al. 2016). Geographically varying environments

100

can generate population structure in the regions of genes involved in adaptation, even under

101

conditions (e.g. high gene flow) that do not generate population structure at a genome-wide level

102

(McKay and Latta 2002). The degree to which population structure influences the patterns of

103

genome-wide LD and therefore the occurrence of false positives has emerged as a critical hurdle

104

(Platt, et al. 2010). To limit the number of spurious associations, studies usually estimate

106

108

2014; Monroe, et al. 2016; Rellstab, et al. 2017; Frachon, et al. 2018; Lasky, et al. 2018; Price, et

112

113

(Forester, et al. 2018), when selection is spatially autocorrelated, accounting for population

114

structure reduces the power to detect loci under selection (Forester, et al. 2018).

115
116

The negative effects of accounting for population structure, may explain the reduced signal of

117

genetic convergence to climate among distantly related conifers (Yeaman, et al. 2016).

118

119

120

2018); and the lack of SNPs showing significant associations to drought survival among

121

Detecting associations to climate with and without accounting for population structure

178
179

To compare allele associations to climate with and without accounting for population structure

180

we focused on the climate variable Minimum Temperature of Coldest Month (Min.Tmp.Cld.M).

181

Using a SNP genotype matrix for a panel of 1,135 globally distributed accessions downloaded

182

from the 1001 Genomes database, we filtered out accessions from outside the native Eurasian

183

and North African range of A. thaliana, as these accessions may have weaker patterns of local

184

adaptation (Lasky, et al. 2012). We also filtered out accessions that were likely laboratory

185

escapees or contaminants (Pisupati, et al. 2017), leaving 875 accessions. After we filtered for

186

biallelic SNPs with minor allele frequency >0.05, we tested association with home climate of

187

ecotype and tested for potential confounding effects of population structure using the software

188

â€œgemmaâ€ (Zhou and Stephens 2012). The parameters used in gemma were a MAF of 0.05

189

(default 0.01) and a missingness threshold of 0.05. For the linear mixed model option, we used

190

Wald test (default) to test for significant associations to climate. We tested models where home

191

climate was a function of SNP allele, and the association p-values we report are for the null

192

hypothesis that the mean climate occupied by the two alleles is equal (Lasky, et al. 2014). Using

193

the same set of SNPs we estimated correlations to climate using simple â€œSpearmanâ€ correlations

194

(Spearman 1987) and not accounting for population structure. To estimate p-values for the

195

Discussion

490

In the quest to study the genetic basis of local adaptation using genome wide associations to

491

environment, linear mixed models have emerged as a powerful tool given their ability to account

492

for population structure while testing for significant associations (Yu, et al. 2006; Kang, et al.

493

2008; Kang, et al. 2010; Zhou and Stephens 2012). Although they provide a robust statistical

494

framework for removing many false positives, the current study shows that such an approach

495

may significantly limit our ability to understand the polygenic basis of local adaptation.

496
497

SNPs showing significant associations to climate after accounting for population structure, were

498

not in line with high F ST and LD SNPs that showed a significant enrichment along fitness QTL

499

peaks and coding/noncoding sites under functional constraint. Since these QTL are of large

500

effect (Ã…gren, et al. 2013), the lack of significant associations is less likely to be a result of many

501

alleles having a small effect on fitness. A more likely explanation is that accounting for

502

population structure using genetic relatedness estimated from genome-wide SNPs, can lead to a

503

significant number of false negatives. Arabidopsis thaliana however, is a simple, highly inbred

504

species â€”to correctly assess the impact of accounting for population structure when examining

505

the genetic basis of local adaptation there needs to be examination of other species with more

506

complex life-history traits and evolutionary dynamics.

507
508

A large portion of SNPs that showed significant associations to climate, and SNPs that showed

509

high F ST and LD between Italy and Sweden populations were enrichment among

24

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/642306; this version posted August 17, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not
certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made available under
aCC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license.

510

nonsynonymous/cis-regulatory variation at sites showing significant functional constraint. These

511

results, further support an important role of cis-regulatory (Lasky, et al. 2014; Siepel and Arbiza

512

2014; Li and Fay 2017; Price, et al. 2018; Sackton, et al. 2019) and nonsynonymous variation

513

(Nachman, et al. 2003; Coop, et al. 2009; Lasky, et al. 2012; Huber, et al. 2014; Svetec, et al.

514

2016; Price, et al. 2018) in adaptation. Among the list of candidate genes underlying fitness QTL

515

and showing significant evidence of local adaptation at functionally constraint sites, we

516

identified FLDH. FLDH is a negative regulator of abscisic-acid signaling (Bhandari, et al. 2010),

517

that showed strong GxE interactions between Italy and Sweden plants under cold acclimation

518

conditions. Abscisic-acid signaling is known to play an important role in abiotic stress response

519

(Tuteja 2007), with many studies supporting its role in local adaptation to climate (Keller, et al.

520

2012; Lasky, et al. 2014; Kalladan, et al. 2017; Ristova, et al. 2017).

521
522

In addition to abscisic-acid signaling, our results provide further support for the important role of

523

flowering time in local adaptation to climate. Among a list of genes that were experimentally

524

shown to affect flowering time, we identified three genes (PIF3, FIO1, and COL5) that showed

525

significant evidence of local adaptation and functional constraint along nonsynonymous sites.

526

FIO1 was previously shown to contain SNPs that showed significant associations to flowering

527

time among natural Swedish lines (Sasaki, et al. 2015) and COL5 was located within a QTL that

528

explains flowering time variation among Sweden and Italy recombinant inbred lines (Ã…gren, et

529

al. 2017). Finally, PIF3, a transcription factor that interacts with phytochromes (Soy, et al.

530

2012), has been implicated in multiple biological processes including early hypocotyl growth

531

(Monte, et al. 2004), photomorphogenesis (Dong, et al. 2017), flowering time (Oda, et al. 2004),

532

and regulation of physiological responses to temperature (Jiang, et al. 2017). This highly

conserved transcription factor was found within a large region that showed significant evidence

When ignoring functional constraint, we identify a list of addition flowering time genes showing

539

540

545

546

547

Finally, the current study shows that we need a new statistical framework to examine genome

549

wide associations to environment, and furthermore, it increases our understanding on the genes

550

and traits that may underlie local adaptation in A. thaliana.
