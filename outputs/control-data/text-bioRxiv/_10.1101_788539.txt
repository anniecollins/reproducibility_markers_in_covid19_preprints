Abstract

4

Spinal cord injury (SCI) results in cell death, demyelination, and axonal loss. The spinal cord has a

5

limited ability to regenerate and current clinical therapies for SCI are not effective in helping promote

6

neurologic recovery. We have developed a novel scaffold biomaterial that is fabricated from the

7

biodegradable hydrogel oligo[poly(ethylene glycol)fumarate] (OPF). We have previously shown that

8

positively charged OPF scaffolds (OPF+) in an open spaced, multichannel design can be loaded with

9

Schwann cells to support axonal generation and functional recovery following SCI. We have now

10

developed a hybrid OPF+ biomaterial that increases the surface area available for cell attachment and that

11

contains an aligned microarchitecture and extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins to better support axonal

12

regeneration. OPF+ was fabricated as 0.08 mm thick sheets containing 100 Œºm high polymer ridges that

13

self-assembles into a spiral shape when hydrated. Laminin, fibronectin, or collagen I coating promoted

14

neuron attachment and axonal outgrowth on the scaffold surface. In addition, the ridges aligned axons in

15

a longitudinal bipolar orientation. Decreasing the space between the ridges increased the number of cells

16

and neurites aligned in the direction of the ridge. Schwann cells seeded on laminin coated OPF+ sheets

17

aligned along the ridges over a 6-day period and could myelinate dorsal root ganglion neurons over 4

18

weeks. The OPF+ sheets support axonal regeneration when implanted into the transected spinal cord.

19

This novel scaffold design, with closer spaced ridges and Schwann cells is a novel biomaterial construct

20

to promote regeneration after SCI.

21

Keywords: neuronal outgrowth, cell attachment, Schwann cells, extracellular matrix, scaffolds, spinal

22

cord injury, axonal regeneration

1. Introduction

3

The spinal cord has a limited ability to regenerate after spinal cord injury (SCI) and available therapies are

4

not efficacious in promoting recovery of motor and sensory neurological function. The failure to recover

5

function may be due to secondary events that occur after the primary insult such as cell death, axonal loss,

6

demyelination, cyst formation and an increase in the inhibitory microenvironment [1]. Many therapies are

7

currently under investigation for treating one or more of these secondary events using neuroprotective

8

strategies and cell, regenerative, and rehabilitative therapies [1, 2]. A single therapeutic strategy is

9

unlikely to be effective in treating a disorder that is heterogeneous in its underlying pathophysiology.

10

Combinatorial treatments that simultaneously address multiple contributions to the residual of the injury

11

will be required.

12

Biomaterial scaffolds are an attractive platform for combinatorial therapies because they are able to bridge

13

the physical gap produced from gliosis, cyst formation, and cell death by providing structural support for

14

regenerating cells [3, 4]. Biomaterials can be combined with extracellular matrices, cells, or

15

pharmaceuticals to promote a more hospitable environment for regeneration [3, 5, 6]. We have developed

16

a positively charged polymer, oligo[poly(ethylene glycol)fumarate] (OPF+) for scaffold fabrication in a

17

multichannel design that overlays the different tracts of the spinal cord [4, 7]. OPF+ forms a porous,

18

biodegradable hydrogel that is mechanically similar to the spinal cord [8]. The positive charge on OPF

19

enhanced neuronal cell attachment, extension and the axonal myelination with Schwann cells in vitro [9].

20

In vivo, OPF+ loaded with Matrigel supported axonal regeneration over 4 weeks [10]. OPF+ scaffolds

21

loaded with Schwann cells further improved axonal regeneration, and growth orientation over that seen

22

with Matrigel alone, and reduced collagen scarring, cyst formation, and proteoglycan accumulation

23

following transection injury [4, 7, 10]. Schwann cells, that were genetically modified to express high

24

concentrations of glial cell-derived neurotrophic factor (GDNF-SC), increased axonal regeneration and

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/788539; this version posted October 1, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not
certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

myelination compared to wild type Schwann cells and led to enhanced functional recovery following

2

spinal cord transection [5].

3

Axonal regeneration occurred through the open channels in the scaffold, as well as on the outer surface of

4

the scaffolds. This observation led to the new hypothesis that changing the scaffold architecture to

5

increase the surface area available for axons to regenerate may increase the number of axons regenerating

6

through the scaffold and provide directional guidance. Different scaffold designs have been fabricated

7

with a higher proportion of open to closed spaces in association with a novel ridged surface architecture

8

that would guide regenerating axons. Physical structures for contact mediated axonal guidance, such as

9

microgrooves or ridges, have been created by laser etching onto polymer surfaces [11-13]. Initial studies

10

using quartz slides microgrooves improved the directional orientation of seeded fibroblast and epithelial

11

cells but did not align chick embryo neurons [11]. Yao et al. (2009) subsequently demonstrated that

12

micro-patterned poly(lactide-co-glycolide) (PLGA) sheets increased neurite alignment. In another study,

13

neonatal rat dorsal root ganglion (DRG) neurons were cultured on grooved polymers of poly(dimethyl

14

siloxane) coated with poly-L-lysine and laminin. On this surface neurites extended along and arched

15

over the grooves with the somas preferentially adhering to the ridges [14].

16

The addition of extracellular matrix (ECM) proteins onto polymer surfaces provides sites for receptor-

17

mediated cell attachment to promote axonal guidance and cell migration [15]. Grooved polymers coated

18

with gradients of laminin or collagen have been shown to direct axonal growth cones [16, 17].

19

Fibronectin promotes Schwann cell proliferation and motility in culture [18], and is an attractive ECM

20

candidate to make biomaterials more cell adhesive and facilitate growth. Peripheral nerve regeneration in

21

rats can be enhanced by the addition of fibronectin and Schwann cells in alginate hydrogels [19].

22

Fibronectin has also been shown to help increase serotonergic axon sprouting and can influence axonal

23

growth when used in combination with cells, growth factors, and biomaterials [20, 21].

1

In this study we sought to determine which ECM molecules best support neuronal attachment, and

2

whether the structural microarchitecture may further improve the directional alignment of axonal

3

extension and myelination in vitro. A novel hybrid OPF+ scaffold design was fabricated as a ridged, flat

4

sheet and coated with ECM proteins. Upon hydration the sheets had the unique property of spontaneously

5

rolling into a spiral 3D configuration. Polymer sheets with ridge spacings of 0.2 mm, 0.4 mm, or 1 mm

6

apart were coated with laminin, collagen, or fibronectin, and used in in vitro assays to quantitate neuronal

7

attachment, DRG neurite extension, and axonal myelination by Schwann cells. A proof of concept study

8

was then conducted in vivo to compare axonal regeneration in laminin and untreated spiral scaffolds

9

implanted following complete spinal cord transection.

3.5 The number of ridges influences Schwann cell organization and axonal myelination.

16

In our previous studies we have loaded the multichannel scaffolds with genetically modified Schwann

17

cells which facilitated axonal regeneration and recovery after spinal cord transection [5]. We next sought

18

to demonstrate that the OPF+ ridged sheet design with laminin coating would support the growth of

19

Schwann cells. Schwann cells on OPF+ ridged sheets initially formed a cell layer in a dispersed and

20

unorganized manner (Figure 5 A-C). Over a 6 day period the Schwann cells organized along the ridges of

21

the scaffold (Figure 5 D-F). When the Schwann cells were co-cultured with dispersed DRG neurons for

22

4 weeks, neurites became myelinated (Figure 6). Neurites aligned well with the ridges and stained

23

positively for myelin basic protein (MBP). Greater densities of aligned myelinated neurites were

24

observed when the ridges were spaced closer together (0.2 mm spacing vs. 1 mm spacing).

A proof of principle pilot experiment was conducted in which uncoated and laminin-coated OPF+ sheets

5

9

potential of our novel rolled scaffold for spinal cord regeneration.

10

4. Discussion

11

We have previously demonstrated that OPF+ fabricated in a multichannel design with seven channels can

12

enhance regeneration after SCI [4, 7] in rats. This regeneration can be further improved by loading the

13

channels with GDNF-secreting Schwann cells, leading to modest functional recovery [5]. We have also

14

shown that the OPF+ material can be modified by incorporating the anti-fibrotic drug rapamycin in PLGA

15

microspheres [27]. OPF+ multichannel scaffolds containing rapamycin microspheres were also combined

16

with Schwann cells to help promote functional recovery following SCI. It was also noted from these

17

studies that many of the regenerating axons grew on the outer circumferential surface of the scaffold. We

18

therefore hypothesized that axonal growth may preferentially occur on surfaces rather than within open

19

spaces. Maximizing the surface areas available to regenerating axons to extend may further improve the

20

density and directionality of regrowth, and impact upon functional reconnections. In this study, we have

21

now demonstrated, in vitro, that OPF+ fabricated in a flat sheet design with ridges increased the surface

22

area available for growth when compared to the multichannel design scaffold. Providing an attractive

23

ECM protein coat and spacing the ridges closer together improved cell attachment, outgrowth, and

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/788539; this version posted October 1, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not
certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

alignment of DRG explants, neurons, and Schwann cells. We also demonstrated that this scaffold design

2

is feasible to use in vivo.

3

When hydrated, the OPF+ sheets spontaneously form a 3D spiral tube. Hydrogels have been designed to

4

self-roll in previous studies either by introducing reductants to cleave disulfide bonds [28], changes in pH

5

and sacrificial layers [29], or using light responsive inks [30] and photolithography [31]. These ridged

6

scaffolds had swelling ratios comparable to our previous studies [32]. Hydrogels may suffer from low

7

cell attachment due to the hydrated surface layer [33]. This issue could be addressed by coating the

8

hydrogel sheets with collagen, laminin, or fibronectin, all of which are classically established as ECM

9

coatings used to promote neurite outgrowth [6, 13, 34, 35]. The current study shows that DRG explants

10

cultured on laminin coated OPF+ sheets had longer neurite outgrowth than fibronectin or collagen coated

11

sheets. In addition, the outgrowth rate of neurites on laminin coated sheets was faster in the first 24 hours

12

of culture. This can be due to the different amount of proteins used or absorbed into the scaffold.

13

Previous studies have demonstrated concentration dependent differences in cell attachment and

14

outgrowth, however, concentrations used in this study were within range of those used in other studies [6,

15

24, 36, 37]. It is also interesting to note that the DRG outgrowth rate on the fibronectin coated sheets was

16

still increasing at the 24-48 hour time point and may have results in greater outgrowth length at a later

17

time point. When neuronal attachment was measured on or in between the ridges, laminin and fibronectin

18

did equally well. The observed enhancement of neuronal outgrowth and guidance associated with laminin

19

is in keeping with several other studies investigating different substrates for neuronal regeneration [6, 13,

20

35, 38]. ECM proteins or their fragments play an important role in cell adhesion through ligand receptor

21

interactions, and these interactions may be an important component to consider in developing novel

22

biomaterials [39-41]. In addition, the three dimensional structure of ECMs help anchor cells and provide

23

axonal guidance [15]. We show that to maximize the ability of hydrogel scaffolds to anchor neurons and

24

their axons and aid in their outgrowth, ECMs like laminin and fibronectin are necessary to maximize the

25

regenerative capability of the scaffold.

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/788539; this version posted October 1, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not
certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

The combination of a scaffold containing ridges and ECM may mimic what is seen in development where

2

topographical and chemical cues help guide axons. Factors such as stiffness of the substrate can be

3

important in determining the growth patterns of neurons, as shown by the growth of retinal ganglion cell

4

axons towards softer tissue [42]. Laminin and Œ≤-integrin signaling have been found to be important in

5

neuronal polarization and the deletion of Œ≤-integrin results in deficits in axonal development [43]. Radial

6

glia, similar to the ridges of the scaffold, provide topographical cues that orientate the migration of

7

embryonic neurons [44]. Neurite outgrowth from DRG explants and disassociated DRG neurons could be

8

directed in a bidirectional way on ridged surfaces in this study. Ridges may provide important

9

topographical cues, as the majority of neurons preferentially attached on or near the ridges of the scaffold.

10

A similar preference for mechanical guidance cues (microgrooves), rather than on flat spaces between the

11

microgrooves, has previously been observed [13]. Studies investigating the effect of grooves on a

12

polymer surface have shown that axonal alignment and behavior could be influenced by the depth of the

13

grooves, spacing between the grooves, and groove widths [13, 38]. Similarly, in our study, disassociated

14

DRG neurons that grew on the OPF+ sheets with ridges spaced 0.2 mm apart had greater cell attachment

15

to the ridges, alignment, and outgrowth than those grown on 1 mm spaced sheets. One of the goals of the

16

scaffold is to bridge the gap that is created after spinal cord injury. The ability of the scaffold to direct

17

growth in a longitudinal direction and maximize this directed growth is an important consideration.

18

Another approach to enhancing regeneration is to mimic the peripheral nervous system environment. It is

19

well established that the peripheral nervous system has a better ability to regenerate than the central

20

nervous system. This may be due to a more rapid clearance and recycling of myelin debris, more

21

permissive ECM, and a neurotrophic Schwann cell response [45]. The use of Schwann cells with

22

biomaterials recreates a peripheral nervous system environment in the spinal cord, where the cells can

23

contribute regenerative ECM, growth factors, and facilitate remyelination. Our lab, among others, has

24

compared different cell types. Schwann cells have been found to be best able to support axonal

25

regeneration [10, 26, 46, 47]. In this study, Schwann cells preferentially organized along the ridges of

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/788539; this version posted October 1, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not
certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

1

OPF+ sheets and myelinated neurites. The OPF+ sheet with ridges spaced 0.2 mm apart had more aligned

2

myelinated neurites observable per area than the 1 mm sheet. This combination of the OPF+ ridged sheet

3

with an ECM and Schwann cells may increase the regenerative potential of the scaffold and these in vitro

4

results suggest that this combination would be helpful in guiding and myelinating aligned axons.

5

This study demonstrates a novel hybrid design of the OPF+ scaffold for use in regenerative medicine,

6

particularly suited for spinal cord injury. It has properties similar to the spinal cord, maximizes available

7

surface area and volume for regeneration, and can spontaneously roll to make a 3D structure for

8

transplantation. Flattened, ridged sheets were used for in vitro studies and demonstrated improved neuron

9

attachment, axonal alignment, and outgrowth. Furthermore, it would be straightforward to manufacture

10

and scale for large animal studies and clinical applications. Scaffold properties can be modified including

11

varying the ridge distance and size, as well as, coating with different substances. It is also possible to

12

study different cell types for different applications in regenerative medicine. In a small study when

13

implanted into transected spinal cord, the rolled scaffold integrated well and supported regenerating

14

axons. This scaffold is well positioned to be used for combinatorial treatments to treat spinal cord injury

15

through use of novel biomaterials, ECMs, and cells.
