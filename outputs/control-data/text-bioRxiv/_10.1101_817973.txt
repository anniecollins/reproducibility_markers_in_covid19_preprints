Abstract
Hyperactivity/impulsivity and inattention are core symptoms dimensions in attention-

3

deficit/hyperactivity disorder. Some approaches suggest that these symptoms arise

4

from deficits in the ability to anticipate and process rewards. However, evidence is

5

equivocal with regard to ADHD-related differences in brain activity during reward

6

processing. The aim of this study was to investigate when, and how, reward-related

7

ERP activity was associated with hyperactive/impulsive symptoms and inattention

8

symptoms. Adults with ADHD (n=34) and matched comparison participants (n=36)

9

completed an electrophysiological version of the Monetary Incentive Delay task. This

10

task separates reward processing into two stages- namely, an anticipation stage and

11

a delivery stage. During the anticipation stage, visual cues signalled a possible

12

monetary incentive (i.e. a reward or loss). After a brief delay, the delivery stage began,

13

and

14

Electroencephalogram activity was simultaneously sampled and incentive-related

15

event relate potentials (ERPs) calculated. These data were then analysed by

16

calculating multiple regression models, at each sample point, wherein the correlation

17

between incentive-related ERPs and ADHD symptoms was estimated. Linear and

18

curvilinear associations between ERP activity and ADHD symptoms were tested in

19

each regression mode. Findings suggest that ADHD symptoms were associated ERP

20

activity at different reward processing stages. Hyperactive/impulsive symptoms were

21

associated with reduced ERP activity during the initial anticipation of rewards from

22

224-329 ms post-reward cue. Inattention symptoms were associated with reduced

23

ERP activity during the initial delivery of rewards from 251-280 ms post-reward onset.

24

Finally, extreme ends of hyperactive/impulsive and inattention symptoms were

25

associated with reduced ERP activity towards the end of the anticipation stage from

26

500 ms post-reward cue onwards. These results support the idea that reward

incentives

were

delivered

contingent

on

a

speeded

button-press.

Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental

31

disorder characterized by patterns of impulsivity, hyperactivity and/or inattention (1).

32

Although ADHD is typically diagnosed in childhood, behavioural symptoms continue

33

into adulthood for most individuals, with an overall prevalence rate of 3-4% (2-5).

34

Recent etiological models posit that ADHD symptoms result from a diminished ability

35

to anticipate rewards (6-8). According to these approaches, ADHD symptoms accrue

36

from lower phasic dopaminergic activity in response to reward-predictive cues (9). This

37

view is supported by evidence of ventral striatum (VS) hypoactivity in response to cues

38

that signal monetary rewards in both adult and adolescent ADHD, relative to healthy

39

comparisons (10-13). Despite these findings, however, the relationship between

40

reward processing and ADHD remains unclear. Furthermore, it is unknown whether

41

deficits in reward processing mediate specific symptoms dimensions in ADHD (e.g.

42

impulsivity and/or inattention) or the disorder in general.

43

Conflicting evidence regarding the relationship between reward processing and

44

ADHD symptoms has been reported using functional magnetic resonance imaging

45

(fMRI) and the monetary incentive delay (MID) task (14). The MID task separates

46

reward processing into two discrete stages. Across trials, a visual cue first signals a

47

possible monetary incentive: the anticipation stage. After a brief period, the incentive

48

is then delivered on a partial reinforcement-schedule, contingent on a response to a

49

target: the delivery stage. A number of studies report a negative association between

50

VS

51

52

results imply that inefficient reward anticipation underlies this specific ADHD symptom

53

dimension. However, the opposite pattern has also been found. In a recent study with

54

a large sample of adolescents, the authors reported a negative association between

activity

in

response

to

cues

that

predict

monetary

rewards

59

60

The lack of consensus regarding ADHD-related deficits during reward

61

processing may partly stem limits inherent in previous FMRI research. While spatially

62

informative, fMRI lacks the temporal resolution to study the transient neuro-cognitive

63

process that mediate each stage of the MID task. Indeed, the stages of reward

64

processing (such as anticipation and delivery) can be further divided into brief

65

cognitive mechanisms like cue detection, attentional allocation, motor-response

66

preparation and evaluative appraisal (19). It is possible that ADHD symptoms relate

67

to atypical patterns of activity during these discrete neuro-cognitive mechanisms

68

involved in reward processing. Therefore, alternative approaches that afford greater

69

temporal granularity are warranted.

70

An electrophysiological (EEG) version of the MID task (e-MID) represents one

71

such promising approach. This task uses event-related potentials (ERPs; time-locked

72

electroencephalograms) to precisely quantify the temporal dynamics of reward

73

processing (15, 19-22). Two ERP components are particularly relevant during the

74

anticipation stage: cue-P3 and contingent negative variation (CNV). Cue-P3

75

amplitudes occur in response to predictive cues, with a peak near 300 ms that is

76

maximal at posterior scalp spaces. Cue-P3 amplitudes are thought to reflect

77

attentional allocation (19-21). CNV ERPs are negative-going potential shifts observed

78

1000-2000 ms after the cue-P3 and are maximal at frontal scalp spaces. These

79

amplitudes are indicative of motor-response preparation. Importantly, the amplitudes

88

89

90

91

92

93

Another important consideration is the possible curvilinear association between

94

ADHD symptoms and activity in reward-related brain areas. While there is evidence

95

of VS hypoactivity in individuals diagnosed with ADHD relative to comparison groups

96

97

98

99

100

101

102

103

association. One reason is that between-group designs, which compare high and low

104

107

No previous study has examined reward processing in adult ADHD using the

108

e-MID task. We therefore administered this task to a sample of adults with a wide

109

range of ADHD symptoms. We aimed to determine specific time points when ERP

110

activity was associated with hyperactive/impulsive and inattention symptoms that are

111

characteristic of with ADHD. We analysed the data continuously using regression and

112

tested for both linear and curvilinear associations between ADHD symptoms and ERP

113

activity. Findings revealed that hyperactive/impulsive and inattention symptoms were

114

associated with reduced ERP activity during different stages of reward processing.

Discussion

397

Reward processing in adult ADHD was explored using the e-MID task. We

398

investigated when, and how, incentive-related ERP activity was associated ADHD

399

symptom dimensions (i.e. hyperactive/impulsive and inattentive symptoms).

400

Specifically, we examined event-related potential (ERP) components that are

401

assumed to reflect brief neuro-cognitive mechanisms involved in the processing,

402

storage and retrieval of reward-relevant information (e.g., cue detection, motor-

403

response preparation and outcome evaluation (19, 21)).

404

We first investigated associations between incentive-related ERP activity and

405

hyperactive/impulsive symptoms. Cue-P3 amplitudes are linked to early top-down

406

processes including attentional allocation (15, 20-22) as well as the updating of working

407

memory in response to new stimuli (19). Motivationally salient cues exert greater

408

demands on top-down processing and this is reflected by higher cue-P3 amplitude

409

(especially for reward cues) (19, 23). In this study, we found a negative association

410

between cue-P3 amplitudes in response to reward cues (around 200 ms post-cue; left

411

posterior-lateral scalp) and impulsivity symptoms. This association was linear,

412

suggesting that reward anticipation is hypoactive in individuals with elevated

413

hyperactive/impulsive symptoms. This pattern may be characteristic of impaired

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/817973; this version posted October 25, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which
was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted bioRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity. It is made
available under aCC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license.

414

reward anticipation. If the transfer of brain activity from rewards to their predictive cues

415

is diminished (i.e., impaired reward anticipation), then predictive cues will place fewer

416

demands on top-down processing. As a result, lower cue-P3 potentiation in response

417

to reward-cues would be expected. Importantly, an attentional deficit cannot explain

418

these findings, because cue-P3 amplitudes in response to neutral cues were not

419

significantly associated with symptoms any point in time.

420

CNV amplitudes in response to reward and loss-cues were significantly

421

associated with hyperactive/impulsive symptoms (around 1500 ms post cue; medial

422

and right posterior-lateral scalp). The relationship between reward-related CNV

423

amplitudes and hyperactive/impulsive symptoms was typical-u-shaped. It is important

424

to note that greater negativity in CNV amplitudes reflects greater motor-response

425

preparation. A typical-u-shaped relation therefore indicates that the extreme ends of

426

hyperactive/impulsive symptoms are associated with smaller CNV amplitudes â€“ that

427

is, poorer motor-response preparation. Interestingly, the opposite pattern was

428

observed for loss-related CNV amplitudes. The association between loss-related CNV

429

amplitudes and hyperactive/impulsive symptoms was an inverted-u-shape. These

430

findings suggest, in the context of motor-response preparation, that the extreme ends

431

of hyperactive/impulsive symptoms are characterised not only by a hyposensitivity to

432

potential rewards but also a hypersensitivity to potential losses. To our knowledge, our

433

study is the first to report this outcome.

434

We also investigated the relationship between incentive-related ERP activity

435

and inattentive symptoms. CNV amplitudes during reward anticipation were

436

associated with inattentive symptoms. Again, this relationship was u-shaped,

437

suggesting that ADHD symptoms in general are linked to disruptions in incentive-

438

related motor response preparation. Specifically, the extreme ends of ADHD

Inattention, but not hyperactive/impulsive symptoms, was associated with

442

negative association between early ERP amplitudes and inattention (around 250 ms,

fronto-central scalp ROI). Recent findings suggest that ERPs at this time and scalp

445

446

been referred to as a reward positivity (RewP) component, which positively increases

447

in response to relative gains (24, 25). For example, Becker and colleagues

448

demonstrated higher ERP positive amplitudes for reward relative to loss and neutral

449

outcomes (32). Furthermore, reward-related positivity was specifically associated with

450

a relative increase in VS, mid-cingulate and mid-frontal cortices [34]. While the exact

451

cognitive significance of the RewP component is unclear, its sensitivity to reward

452

outcomes appears to reflect some an aspect of evaluative (or motivational) processing

453

(19). Following on from this, our findings suggest that inattention symptoms, in the

454

context ADHD, are associated with a disruption in the evaluative processing of reward-

Our findings differ from the only other investigation of reward processing in

468

469

470

471

anticipation is hypersensitive in ADHD. Although the precise reason for these

472

contradicting results is unclear, there are some methodological differences between

Some etiological models suggest that ADHD symptoms result from diminished

492

493
