Abstract

25

Food technologies provide numerous benefits to society and are extensively vetted for safety.

26

However, many technological innovations still face high levels of skepticism from consumers.

27

To promote development and use of food technologies, it is critical to understand the

28

psychological and neurobiological processes associated with consumer acceptability concerns.

29

The current study uses a neuroscience-based approach to understand consumer attitudes and

30

perceptions of risk associated with food technologies and investigate how such attitudes impact

31

consumerâ€™s processing of information related to food technologies. We used functional magnetic

32

resonance imaging (fMRI) to measure brain activation while participants processed infographics

33

related to food technology topics. For technology topics perceived as riskier (antibiotics and

34

hormones), activation was higher in areas of the lateral prefrontal cortex that are associated with

35

decisional uncertainty. In contrast, technology topics that were viewed more favorably

36

(sustainability and animal welfare) tended to activate the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, a region

37

that processes positive affect and subjective value. Moreover, for information about hormones,

38

the lateral PFC activation was associated with individual differences in resistance to change in

39

risk perception. These results reveal how attitudes and risk perception relate to how the brain

40

processes information about food technologies and how people respond to information about

41

such technologies.

1. Introduction
Innovations in food technology hold great promise for improving food quality,

48

availability, safety, and sustainability. However, like other technological innovations (Kleijnen,

49

Lee, & Wetzelsa, 2009), new food technologies are often met with resistance in the marketplace.

50

Consumers tend to be neophobic when it comes to foods (Pliner & Hobden, 1992), particularly

51

foods produced with new technologies (Cox & Evans, 2008), even when expert scientists have

52

vetted the technologies for safety (Hansen, Holm, Frewer, Robinson, & SandÃ¸e, 2003). Such

53

neophobia presents a barrier to development of novel technologies as well as their adoption by

54

food producers. The high cost of investment in bringing technologies to market or deploying

55

such technologies in food production can become financially risky when faced with the high

56

potential for rejection by consumers (Lusk, Roosen, & Bieberstein, 2014). Thus, to promote

57

development of safe, effective technologies for improving food sustainability and safety, it is

58

critical to understand the psychological and neurobiological factors associated with consumer

59

attitudes and risk perception for new technologies.

60

Consumer research on food technologies has uncovered several factors that influence

61

acceptance and resistance to innovation, as well as peopleâ€™s willingness-to-pay for such

62

innovations in the marketplace (Lusk et al., 2014). At a proximal level, peopleâ€™s attitudes and

63

perceptions of risk play a critical role in influencing acceptance of new food technologies.

64

Compared to organic or â€œnaturalâ€ foods, foods produced with technologies are often viewed as

65

having fewer benefits and higher risks (Frewer, Shepherd, & Sparks, 1994; Saba, Rosati, &

66

Vassallo, 2000). Thus, consumers are often willing to pay more to avoid technologies such as

67

genetically modified organisms (GMOs), hormones, or antibiotics than they are to purchase food

68

created with them (Lusk et al., 2015; Lusk et al., 2014; Roosen et al., 2015). The origins of

94

The lateral PFC tends to track measures of economic risk and uncertainty, which are related to

95

variability in possible outcomes associated with a decision (Kahnt, Heinzle, Park, & Haynes,

96

2011; Schonberg, Fox, & Poldrack, 2011). It typically activates more when a variety of different

97

98

99

100

101

102

103

In contrast to the lateral PFC, the medial PFC, particularly the ventral part of it (the

104

underside; ventromedial PFC), tends to track the overall subjective value of a decision (Levy &

105

Glimcher, 2012; Bartra, McGuire, & Kable, 2013). When a decision has high subjective value,

106

this brain region tends to activate more, and when there is low subjective value, it activates less.

107

The medial PFC tracks individual differences in preference for immediate reward (Kable &

108

Glimcher, 2007) and sensitivity to gains versus losses (Tom, Fox, Trepel, & Poldrack, 2007).

109

Whereas the lateral PFC is often depicted as a â€œcold,â€ rational part of the brain, the medial part is

110

111

112

113

in basic regulation of cravings and emotional responses (Delgado, Gillis, & Phelps, 2008; Kober

114

et al., 2010; Kahathuduwa, Boyd, Davis, Oâ€™Boyle, & Binks, 2016; Kahathuduwa et al., 2018).

Following general neuroeconomic research, fMRI research on the neurobiological

121

122

With the relatively limited neuroscience research on how people perceive food

138

technologies, there are many open questions regarding how the brain processes information

139

about food technology and how these processes influence potential consumer behavior. Given

140

the critical role that information and education about technologies plays in consumer acceptance,

141

one important question is how the brain processes information about food technologies and how

142

this processing relates to perceived risk and consumer attitudes. Perceived risk and attitudes are

143

key pre-decisional factors that influence not only how consumers will behave when given a

144

choice between foods produced with new technologies or more â€œnaturalâ€ foods, but also how

145

receptive they will be to new information (Hart et al., 2009; Sears & Freedman, 1967; Smith,

146

Fabrigar, & Norris, 2008).

147

In the present study, we scanned individuals while they viewed infographics describing

148

six food technology topics: hormones, antibiotics, vaccines, GMOs, animal welfare technologies,

149

and sustainability technologies (for task design see Figure 1; Supplemental Figures 1-6). The

150

infographics were selected from materials designed for marketing purposes and are publicly

151

152

health-usa.com/about/us/veterinary-consumer-affairs/animal-welfare). Thus, the infographics

153

used are highly ecologically valid in the sense that they are from actual informational campaigns

154

aimed at influencing consumer acceptance. Based on an online norming study (described below),

155

156

hormones and antibiotics would be perceived as most risky and associated with less positive

157

attitudes, GMOs and vaccines as intermediate, and animal welfare and sustainability

158

technologies as least risky and associated with the most positive attitudes.

4. Discussion

469

Food technologies play an important role in food quality, sustainability, and global food

470

security, yet technological innovation is often met with resistance from consumers. The present

471

study used neuroimaging to reveal how the brain processes information related to food

472

technology topics and how these neurobiological processes vary with perceived riskiness and

473

attitudes associated with the technologies. Participants viewed infographics about six different

474

food technology topics including antibiotics, hormones, GMOs, vaccines, sustainability, and

475

animal welfare. We found that activation in lateral PFC tracked between-infographic differences

476

in risk perception and attitudes and was most activated for the higher perceived risk / less

477

positive attitude topics (antibiotics and hormones). In contrast, ventromedial PFC activation

478

tracked positive attitudes and was most activated for the topics (GMOs, vaccines, sustainability,

479

and animal welfare) associated with more positive attitudes. In addition to our primary, between-

480

infographic results, we found individual differences in risk perception change for hormones was

The fMRI results present a critical step forward in understanding consumer attitudes and

Nevertheless, our primary goal of examining how risk perception and attitudes relate to brain

607

processing during infographic viewing is not dependent on participants changing how they

608

processed the infographics based on the cues. For example, even if all infographic viewing

609

blocks were processed the same (or if there were no cues presented at all), our findings that brain

610

activation during infographic processing tracks subsequent between-infographic differences in

611

risk perception and attitudes presents a useful starting point for studying how information about

612

food technologies is processed by the brain.

613

A final limitation of the present study is that the infographics we employed contained a

614

wide range of varied strategies to convey the safety and utility of the food technologies. Thus, it

615

is not possible to separately conclude which aspects of the infographics may have been stronger

616

or weaker in influencing attitudes and risk perception, or brain activation, the most. For example,

617

the hormones infographic contains information highlighting the low tissue accumulation of

618

hormones from hormone implants, relative to other dietary sources of estrogen, information
