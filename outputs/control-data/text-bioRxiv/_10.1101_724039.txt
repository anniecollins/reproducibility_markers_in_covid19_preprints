Abstract

29

During central nervous system (CNS) development, spatiotemporal gene

30

expression programs mediate specific lineage decisions to generate neuronal

31

and glial cell types from neural stem cells (NSCs). However, little is known

32

about the epigenetic landscape underlying these highly complex developmental

33

events. Here, we perform ChIP-seq on distinct subtypes of Drosophila FACS-

34

purified neural stem cells (NSCs) and their differentiated progeny to dissect the

35

epigenetic changes accompanying the major lineage decisions in vivo. By

36

analyzing active and repressive histone modifications, we show that stem cell

37

identity genes are silenced during differentiation by loss of their activating

38

marks and not via repressive histone modifications. Our analysis also uncovers

39

a new set of genes specifically required for altering lineage patterns in type II

40

neuroblasts, one of the two main Drosophila NSC identities. Finally, we

41

demonstrate that this subtype specification in NBs, unlike NSC differentiation,

42

requires Polycomb-group (PcG)-mediated repression.

Introduction

45

During development of the central nervous system (CNS), neural stem cells

46

(NSCs) divide asymmetrically to generate daughter cells with self-renewing

47

capacity but also complex neurogenic and gliogenic lineages. Regulation of this

48

process requires tightly and highly dynamic control of multiple cell fate

49

decisions. For cells to commit to their ultimate cell identity, spatiotemporal gene

50

expression programs are required. It is assumed that activation of lineage-

51

specific genes and silencing of stem cell genes is accompanied by changes in

52

chromatin states. How histone modifications change over time during

53

neurogenesis in vivo, however, is not very well described.

54

The Drosophila larval CNS has become a key model for the fundamental

55

mechanisms underlying brain development and chromatin states (Homem &

56

Knoblich, 2012). The larval CNS is populated by distinct types of NSCs (called

57

neuroblasts in Drosophila), which vary in abundance, neuronal output and

58

division mode. Together, these NBs give rise to the majority of the adult brain‚Äôs

59

neurons (Truman & Bate, 1988). The majority of the central brain NBs are of

60

Type I (NBIs). Each NBI gives rise to another NBI and a ganglion mother cell

61

(GMC), which divides once more to generate two differentiated neurons of glia.

62

Type II neuroblasts (NBIIs) instead are a rare subpopulation with only 8 NBII

63

per brain lobe (Fig. 1A) (Bello, Izergina, Caussinus, & Reichert, 2008; Boone &

64

Doe, 2008; Homem & Knoblich, 2012; Sousa-Nunes, Cheng, & Gould, 2010).

65

Unlike NBIs, NBIIs divide into one NBII and one transit-amplifying cell called

66

intermediate neural progenitors (INPs). They generate many more neurons,

67

because INPs continue to divide asymmetrically for 5-6 times, each time giving

68

rise to a GMC that divides into two neurons or glia cells. (Bello et al., 2008;

69

Boone & Doe, 2008; Homem & Knoblich, 2012). Other than lineage structure

70

and size, cell markers can also be used to differentiate NB subtypes. While

71

NBIs express both Asense (Ase) and Deadpan (Dpn) (Bowman et al., 2008),

72

NBIIs only express Dpn (Bello et al., 2008). During neurogenesis, both NB

73

subtypes divide asymmetrically to give rise to their respective progeny (Kang &

74

Reichert, 2014). Brain tumors form if the asymmetric segregation of cell fate

determinants during NB cell division is disrupted (Betschinger, Mechtler, &

Knoblich, 2006; Juergen A Knoblich, 2010). Among these determinants are the

77

TRIM-NHL protein Brain tumor (Brat) and the Notch inhibitor Numb (Arama,

differentiated brain cells (Bowman et al., 2008), simultaneous loss of Brat and

83

Numb causes the NBI-like tNBs to overproliferate (see Results).

84

In many cell types, transitions in chromatin states are regulated by the

85

evolutionary conserved Polycomb (PcG) and Trithorax (TrxG) group proteins.

86

PcG and TrxG have emerged as antagonistic regulators that silence or activate

87

gene expression, respectively (Kingston & Tamkun, 2014; Levine et al., 2002;

88

Schuettengruber, Chourrout, Vervoort, Leblanc, & Cavalli, 2007). These

89

multimeric protein complexes regulate the transcriptional state of genes by

90

post-translationally modifying amino acid residues of histone tails (Kingston &

91

Tamkun, 2014; Levine, King, & Kingston, 2004). PcG proteins exert a

92

repressive activity via two main complexes, the Polycomb repressive

93

complexes 1 and 2 (PRC1 and PRC2). Although PRC1 and PRC2 can exist in

94

various compositions and associate with context-specific accessory proteins,

95

both PRC1 and PRC2 have been shown to contain a specific core set of

96

proteins including subunits with catalytic activity (Bracken, Dietrich, Pasini,

97

Hansen, & Helin, 2006; Simon & Kingston, 2009). Within PRC2, Enhancer of

98

zeste (E(z) in Drosophila, EZH1/2 in mammals) catalyzes the trimethylation of

99

lysine 27 on histone 3 (H3K27me3) (Cao & Zhang, 2004). H3K27me3 is

100

recognized by PRC1, which in turn includes the histone 2A ubiquityltransferase

101

Sce (RING1A/B in mammals) (de Napoles et al., 2004). Histone modifications

102

associated with active transcription are deposited by TrxG proteins (Kassis,

103

Kennison, & Tamkun, 2017), which counteract repressive histone acetylation

104

or methylation marks, in particular by trimethylation of lysine 4 on histone 3 at

105

active promoters (Byrd & Shearn, 2003; Dou et al., 2005; Petruk et al., 2001)

106

(Kim et al., 2005).

107

Although well-known for their role in long-term transcriptional memory, PcG and

108

TrxG complexes are highly dynamic during development and thus facilitate

109

cellular plasticity (Kwong et al., 2008; Negre et al., 2006). In the last decade, it

110

has been shown that PcG and TrxG complexes are crucial to ensure correct

111

neurogenesis in mammals (Hirabayashi et al., 2009; Lim et al., 2009; Pereira

112

et al., 2010) as well as in Drosophila (Bello, Holbro, & Reichert, 2007; Touma,

113

Weckerle, & Cleary, 2012). Despite the strength of genetic in vivo experiments,

114

however, global analysis of the histone modifications underlying their function,

115

and therefore target genes, has mainly been performed in vitro. This constitutes

116

a real knowledge-gap as recent studies demonstrated that the chromatin states

117

may vary significantly between in vivo tissues and their related in vitro cell lines,

118

mainly due to culture conditions (R. Xie et al., 2013; Zhu et al., 2013). Given

119

also that epigenetic changes are highly context ‚Äì and developmental time-

120

dependent, providing in vivo datasets to investigate chromatin states of different

121

cell types in complex tissues will increase our understanding of how the

122

epigenetic landscape dynamically defines cellular states.

123

In recent years, in vivo studies made use of Drosophila to shed light on the

124

dynamics of chromatin state changes during embryonic neural differentiation

125

(Ye et al., 2016) and during larval stages (Aughey, Estacio-G√≥mez, Thomson,

126

Yin, & Southall, 2018; Marshall & Brand, 2017). Profiling the binding of

127

chromatin remodelers has highlighted the plasticity of chromatin states during

128

differentiation (Marshall & Brand, 2017). Although binding of chromatin factors

129

is associated with active or repressive chromatin, binding does not necessarily

130

reflect downstream histone modifications. For example, the histone marks can

131

change drastically between parasegments of the Drosophila embryo while the

132

occupancy of PcG proteins remains unchanged (Bowman et al., 2014). Thus,

133

investigating the dynamics of chromatin states based on chromatin marks is

134

crucial for understanding the functional specialization of cells during

135

development. Moreover, how PcG/TrxG complexes target genes on the

136

chromatin level between different subtypes of progenitor cells during neuronal

137

differentiation, or tumorigenic transformation has remained elusive.

Here, we use the Drosophila larval CNS to track in vivo changes of histone

139

modifications not only upon differentiation, but also between different

140

populations of neural stem cells and their tumorigenic counterparts. We

141

developed a FACS-based method to sort different cell types and perform ChIP-

142

Seq for the active histone mark, H3K4me3, and the repressive mark,

143

H3K27me3. Our FACS-based approach provides an in vivo dataset that reveals

144

dynamic histone modifications during neuronal differentiation. In particular, we

145

observed that self-renewal and cell division genes are repressed independently

146

of H3K27me3 levels. In contrast, we further show that H3K27me3-mediated

147

repression is crucial for silencing lineage-specific stem cell factors, including

148

known factors as wells as a new set of genes that are specific to NBIIs. Finally,

149

we present genetic evidence for the requirement of these new NBII-specific

150

factors for self-renewal and demonstrate the role of PcG complexes in defining

151

different subtypes of neural stem cells.

153

Profiling repressive and active histone modifications of neural stem cells

154

and neurons.

155

H3K4me3 and H3K27me3 are two major histone modifications associated with

156

TrxG-activated and PcG-repressed states, respectively. However, these

157

histone modifications have not yet been analyzed independently in distinct

158

subtypes of neural stem cells in Drosophila. To analyze H3K4me3 and

159

H3K27me3 histone marks in different brain cell types by ChIP-Seq, we

160

combined genetic labeling with a protocol for generating sequencing libraries

161

from picogram quantities of DNA (Bowman et al., 2014). The NB subtype-

162

specific GAL4 drivers ase-GAL4 (NBI lineage-specific) and wor-GAL4, ase-

163

GAL80 (NBII lineage-specific), allowed us to preferentially label distinct NB

164

lineages with nuclear-localized fluorophores (stinger::GFP or RFP). Indeed,

165

GFP expressed by ase-GAL4 exclusively labeled NBI lineages (positive for both

166

Dpn and Ase) and was not expressed in NBIIs (only Dpn+) (Fig. 1B). To amplify

167

the production of rare NBIIs and at the same time generate tumor NBs (tNBs),

168

RNAi constructs against the cell fate determinants brat and numb, were

169

expressed using the mentioned driver lines. Depletion of brat in larval brains

170

with a NBII-specific driver resulted in the overproliferation of NBII-like tNBs,

171

evident by an increase in Dpn+, Ase- cells (Fig. 1C). In contrast, simultaneously

172

depleting numb and brat by an NBI-specific driver resulted in overgrowth of

173

(Dpn+, Ase+) NBI-like tNBs (Fig. 1B). Therefore, this strategy allowed us to

174

generate fluorescently labeled distinct NB cell types and neurons.

175

Besides central brain NB lineages, the larval brain consists of embryonic

176

neurons, mushroom body neuroblasts and cells of the optic lobes. To avoid

177

impurities from these structures NBI, neurons and tNBs were isolated according

178

to fluorescence intensity and cell size by flow cytometry (Berger et al., 2012;

179

Harzer, Berger, Conder, Schmauss, & Knoblich, 2013). Purified cell populations

180

were then analyzed by ChIP-seq for H3K27me3 and H3K4me3 histone

181

modifications (Fig. 1D).

Discussion

363

We provide a resource of histone modification datasets for different types of

364

neural stem cells and their differentiated progeny. In combination with

365

chromatin accessibility (Aughey et al., 2018) and binding maps of chromatin

366

remodelers (Marshall & Brand, 2017) of Drosophila brain cells, we hope that

367

our dataset will serve as an useful community resource. We show that during

368

differentiation, stem cell identity genes are silenced in a PcG-independent

369

manner, which supports previous findings showing that these genes are

370

silenced through HP1 enriched chromatin (Marshall & Brand, 2017).

371

Additionally, PcG-mediated silencing is unlikely to instruct the stepwise

372

inactivation of stem cell genes during differentiation as loss of H3K27me3 did

373

not induce ectopic NBs.

374

Here, we take advantage of in vivo genetic labeling to investigate chromatin

375

dynamics of different NB subtypes. As the type II NBs are very lowly abundant,

376

we used tumor NBs of type I and type II origins as a proxy in order to obtain

377

enough material to be able to compare these two cell types. We further

378

validated each change observed by comparing tumor to healthy type I NBs and

379

excluded artifacts due to the tumorigenic state of the cells. Our data show that

380

both TrxG and PcG are required to establish NBII identity. We identify a set of

381

NBII-specific genes, including previously identified btd (Komori et al., 2014) and

382

Sp1 (√Ålvarez & D√≠az-Benjumea, 2018). We further identified Dll and eya which

383

are specifically required for NBII maintenance. It has been previously described

384

that btd acts as an activator of Dll in the development of the ventral imaginal

385

discs (Estella, Rieckhof, Calleja, & Morata, 2003). This suggests that in NBII-

386

identity specification the Trithorax-target btd could act together with Dll and eya.

387

Such a mechanism would explain why the loss of btd causes a distinct

388

phenotype compared to the loss of Dll and eya. Interestingly, a NBI to NBII

389

conversion is observed only in 18% of NBIs ectopically over-expressing btd

390

indicating that either cofactors are missing or that the chromatin of btd targets

391

is inaccessible (Komori et al., 2014). Our data of NB subtype-specific genes

392

being characterized by H3K27me3 repressive chromatin favor the latter.

14

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/724039; this version posted August 3, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was
not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

393

Therefore, as opposed to TrxG-activated stem cell and mitosis genes, the

394

repression of NBII-specific genes is ensured by PcG-mediated H3K27me3

395

histone modifications suggesting that Polycomb plays a role in defining the

396

diversity of neural stem cell lineages. Moreover, our data indicates that PcG

397

repression is required not only for the silencing of HOX genes but also for the

398

self-renewal capacity of NBs. Unlike TrxG (Komori et al., 2014), the loss of

399

catalytic subunits of PcG complexes did not convert NBIIs to NBIs or vice versa.

400

This suggests that NB subtype-specification cannot be explained solely by an

401

absence of repression but requires a further activation mechanism. Strikingly,

402

loss of PcG complexes caused a significant decrease in the numbers of NBs.

403

Interestingly, across all the cell types, developmental genes such as cad, eve,

404

peb, scr, and slp1, as well as genes involved in embryonic NB temporal

405

patterning (hb, kr, pdm, cas and grh), are heavily marked with H3K27me3. It is

406

therefore possible that PcG-mediated repression is required to silence these

407

developmentally crucial genes in addition to the HOX genes. Thus, the

408

observed reduction in NB stemness might be caused by the de-repression of

409

these genes.

410

Besides an overall decreased NB maintenance, we observed increased

411

sensitivity of NBII lineages upon reduction of PRC2 activity. Interestingly, opa

412

and ham, two previously described temporal switch genes in NBII lineages

413

(Abdusselamoglu, Eroglu, Burkard, & Knoblich, 2019), are also enriched with

414

H3K27me3 in NBs. Ectopic expression of these genes limits self-renewal of

415

NBs and causes NBs to disappear (Abdusselamoglu et al., 2019; Eroglu et al.,

416

2014). In the future, investigating the downstream targets of PcG in different

417

NB subtypes could reveal the underlying mechanisms of subtype-specification.

418

In conclusion, our data provide a useful resource to investigate how chromatin

419

state dynamics orchestrate the diversity and correct progression of neural stem

420

cell lineages.

Preparation of soluble chromatin

461

50000 sorted cells of interest were pelleted by centrifuging at 3000 rpm for 10
