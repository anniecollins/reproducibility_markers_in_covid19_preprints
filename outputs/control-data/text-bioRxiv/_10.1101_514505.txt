Abstract
Using contextual information to predict aversive events is a critical ability that protects from
generalizing fear responses to safe contexts. Animal models have demonstrated the importance of
spatial context representations within the hippocampal formation in contextualization of fear
learning. The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) is known to play an important role in safety
learning, possibly also through the incorporation of context information. However, if contextual
representations are related to context-dependent expression of fear memory in humans remains
unclear. Twenty-one healthy participants underwent functional MRI combined with a cue-context
conditioning paradigm within a self-navigated virtual reality environment. The environment
included two buildings (Threat and Safe context), which had distinct features outside but were
identical inside. Within each context, participants saw two cues (CS+, CS-). The CS+ was consistently
(100% reinforcement rate) paired with an electric shock in the Threat context, but never in the Safe
context. The CS- was never paired with a shock. We found robust differential skin conductance
responses (SCRs; CS+ > CS-) in the Threat context, but also within the Safe context, indicating fear
generalization. Within the Safe context, vmPFC responses to the CS+ were larger than those in the
Threat context. We furthermore found environment-specific representantions for the two contexts
in the training paradigm (i.e., before conditioning took place) in the hippocampus to be related to fear
expression and generalization. Namely, participants with a weak context representation (z-score <
1.65) showed stronger fear generalization compared to participants with a strong context
representation (z-score > 1.65). Thus, a low neural representation of spatial context may explain
overgeneralization of memory to safe contexts. In addition, our findings demonstrate that contextdependent regulation of fear expression engages ventromedial prefrontal pathways suggesting this
involves a similar mechanism that is known to be involved in retrieval of extinction memory.

Introduction

2

The unwanted expression of learned fear responses in safe environments is one of the hallmarks of

3

anxiety and fear-related disorders (Lissek & Grillon, 2010; Shin & Liberzon, 2010). A crucial factor

4

that protects against this overgeneralization of fear is the ability to correctly use contextual factors

5

to predict threat and regulate fear expression. Animal research has shown that accurate context-

6

dependent prediction of threat depends on spatial context representations within the hippocampal

7

formation (Maren, Phan, & Liberzon, 2013). The ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) is known

8

to be critically involved in regulating fear expression (Dunsmoor & Paz, 2015; Greenberg, Carlson,

9

Cha, Hajcak, & Mujica-Parodi, 2013), but also fear generalization (Xu & S√ºdhof, 2013). In humans, the

10

role of these regions in context-dependent expression and generalization of fear remains to be

11

investigated.

12

The role of context information in fear learning has been mostly studied in animal models

13

using context conditioning paradigms (Maren et al., 2013). In these paradigms, rodents learn that the

14

context (e.g., a box) predicts an aversive outcome, unlike cue conditioning paradigms, in which a cue

15

(e.g., a tone) serves as conditioned stimulus (CS) to predict an unconditioned stimulus (UCS).

16

Different neural mechanisms are involved in associating threat with a cue versus a context. The

17

amygdala plays a critical role in both the formation and expression of the cue CS-UCS association

18

(LeDoux, 2003; Phillips & LeDoux, 1992), although in humans, the role of the amygdala in fear

19

expression is less clear (Fullana et al., 2016; Mechias, Etkin, & Kalisch, 2010). The hippocampus is

20

critically involved in spatial mapping (Epstein, Patai, Julian, & Spiers, 2017; O‚ÄôKeefe & Nadel, 1978)

21

and thereby also plays a role in the acquisition and storage of contextual threat information (Wiltgen,

22

2006). In addition to the context predicting the aversive outcome by itself, the context can also

23

change the predictive value of the cue. In this case, the context serves as an ‚Äúoccasion setter‚Äù (Bouton

24

& Nelson, 1998; Holland & Bouton, 1999; Maren et al., 2013) such that the CS predicts an aversive

25

outcome in one context, but not in another.

26

Electrophysiological studies in rodents have demonstrated the existence of place cells within

27

the hippocampus which selectively increase their firing rates when an animal is at a specific location

28

(O‚ÄôKeefe & Dostrovsky, 1971), thus providing a neuronal representation of the spatial environment

29

(O‚ÄôKeefe & Conway, 1978; Wilson & McNaughton, 1993). In addition to neuronal spikes, hippocampal

30

local field potentials (LFP) have also been shown to code spatial location (Agarwal et al., 2014).

31

Similarly, research with intracranial recordings in humans has shown that cells in the hippocampus

32

respond to specific spatial locations while navigating around a virtual environment (Ekstrom et al.,

33

2003). Also using non-invasive imaging techniques, such as blood oxygen level-dependent functional

magnetic resonance imaging (BOLD-fMRI), evidence was found for spatial representations in medial

35

temporal lobe (MTL) regions in humans (Steemers et al., 2016). Work using machine learning

36

methods applied to BOLD-fMRI data has furthermore shown that it is possible to decode

37

environment-specific representantions within a virtual environment from patterns of hippocampal

38

activity (Hassabis et al., 2009; Rodriguez, 2010; Sulpizio, Committeri, & Galati, 2014). We therefore

39

reasoned that the ability to decode environment-specific representantions from such patterns of

40

hippocampal activity can serve as an index of the strength of a context representation.

41

Context representations might play an important role in fear generalization. Animal research

42

has shown that lesions in the hippocampus prevent contextual fear conditioning in rats (Kim &

43

Fanselow, 1992), providing direct evidence for the importance of the hippocampus for contextual

44

fear learning. In humans, using virtual reality on a computer screen (Dunsmoor, Ahs, Zielinski, &

45

LaBar, 2014) or a head-mounted device (Kroes, Dunsmoor, Mackey, McClay, & Phelps, 2017), it is

46

now possible to study the role of context in fear learning. Initial studies using virtual reality by means

47

of preprogrammed movies have shown that responses to the CS+ acquired in a threat context

48

generalize to a safe context (Baas, Nugent, Lissek, Pine, & Grillon, 2004). However, since volitional

49

movement influences the formation of context representations in rodents (Cei, Girardeau, Drieu,

50

Kanbi, & Zugaro, 2014), these paradigms are less suited to investigate context representations. Here,

51

we therefore used a self-navigated virtual reality environment to test the hypothesis that individuals

52

with a low hippocampal context representation show increased fear generalization in the safe

53

context compared to those with a high context representation.

54

The vmPFC is thought to play a critical role in regulating the expression of fear memory, and

55

has been investigated extensively in the context of extinction leaning (Milad & Quirk, 2012). It has

56

been shown that extinction recall is context dependent (Milad, Orr, Pitman, & Rauch, 2005), and that

57

this context-dependent expression of extinction requires the vmPFC and its interactions with the

58

hippocampus and amygdala (Maren et al., 2013). A failure to recruit the vmPFC in response to safe

59

stimuli has been associated with deficiencies in fear generalization (Cha et al., 2014; Holt, Coombs,

60

Zeidan, Goff, & Milad, 2012). In animal models, the mPFC has been implicated in fear generalization

61

as well (Xu & S√ºdhof, 2013). We reasoned that the presentation of a CS+ in a safe context could lead

62

to a suppression of fear responses similar to a CS+ presentation during extinction recall (Maren et al.,

63

2013) and therefore hypothesized that processing of a cue predicting an aversive outcome presented

64

in a safe context is supported by similar vmPFC-dependent mechanisms as those involved in

65

extinction recall.

The present study was designed to test these hypotheses in humans using fMRI and a virtual

67

reality environment. Twenty-one healthy participants underwent a context training paradigm within

68

the virtual reality environment. This environment involved a rich landscape including two buildings.

69

The buildings had distinct features on the outside, but were identical on the inside. Within this

70

environment, participants underwent context training which was followed by a differential delay

71

contextual fear conditioning paradigm with spatial context as occasion setter. The context training

72

paradigm served to create a strong context representation, and to allow for measurement of these

73

representations without the potential confound of threat-induced arousal during the fear

74

conditioning paradigm. During the fear conditioning paradigm, two different cues (CS+ and CS-) were

75

presented in both buildings, but the CS+ was only paired with a mild electric shock (at a

76

reinforcement rate of 100%) in the threat context, and never in the safe context. The CS- was never

77

paired with a shock. Estimations of spatial context representation strength were obtained using a

78

linear support vector machine (SVM) classifier that we trained on hippocampal patterns obtained

79

from and tested in the context training paradigm.

80

We tested the following predictions. First, we expected that, in addition to differential

81

conditioned SCRs in the threat building, differential conditioned SCRs in the safe building would be

82

present (i.e., where participants never received a shock, indicating fear generalization). Second, we

83

expected that also differential conditioned BOLD responses in the threat context would generalize to

84

the safe context. Third, if context-dependent downregulation of fear expression in safe environments

85

is indeed a process similar to extinction recall, then CS+ in the safe context should elicit stronger

86

BOLD responses in the vmPFC than CS+ in the threat context. Fourth, we predicted using multi-voxel

87

patterns of hippocampal activity that the hippocampus has a distinct representation of the two

88

buildings in the virtual environment. If low representations of spatial context play a role in fear

89

overgeneralization, then participants with lower classifier accuracy in distinguishing the two

90

buildings should exhibit increased generalization of conditioned SCRs the safe context.

Context familiarization of the virtual environment

116

Participants navigated through a virtual environment from a first-person perspective. The

117

environment involved a rich landscape including two buildings which they were able to enter. See

118

Figure 1C for an illustration of the environment. The buildings had distinct features on the outside

119

but were identical on the inside. First, we familiarized participants with the entire virtual

120

environment which was done outside the MRI scanner. In this paradigm, participants navigated

121

through the environment using three buttons (i.e., forward, left, and right) based on instructions that

122

where given to them. For example, participants were instructed to ‚Äúgo to the wooden barrel‚Äù or ‚Äúwalk

123

to the bucket‚Äù. Through these instructions, participants visited all areas of the environment at least

124

once.

125

After this initial familiarization paradigm, the participants practiced the main task. They were

126

placed in the environment and were instructed to walk to an instruction booth located outside of the

127

two main buildings. Once they entered, the number "1" or "2" was displayed, indicating which

128

building they were required to visit. Participants had to figure out which building corresponded to

129

this number. If they stood in front of the door of the correct building, the door would open and they

130

had to enter. In the middle of the building, there was a ‚Äútrial booth‚Äù which they had to enter. At that

131

moment, participants could not navigate anymore and a trial would be presented on the screen in 2D.

132

For this familiarization paradigm, a trial consisted of a fixation cross presented for 4-6 sec. In total,

133

there were 48 trials (24 per building). If after a trial, the next trial was in the same building, the door

134

would stay closed. There were always two to four consecutive trials in the same building to reduce

135

the amount of time spent on navigation. After these trials, the door would be open and participants

136

could leave the building and navigate to the instruction booth, which switched to a new random

137

location each time, but was always outside the two buildings. Participants never visited the same

138

building more than twice in a row.

Discussion
This study was designed to investigate the role of spatial context representations in context-

417

dependent expression and generalization of learned fear. We hypothesized that suppression of the

418

expression of conditioned fear in safe contexts would depend on similar neural mechanisms as those

419

involved in extinction recall. Furthermore, we hypothesized that a low context representation of a

420

virtual environment would underlie the generalization of fear to a safe context. We tested these

421

hypotheses in a contextual fear conditioning paradigm in which context in a self-navigated virtual

422

environment served as an ‚Äúoccasion setter‚Äù.

423

We report the following findings. First, we found expression of differentially conditioned

424

responses (i.e., increased SCRs to the CS+ compared to the CS-) in a safe context (i.e., where

425

participants never received a shock), demonstrating generalization of fear and thus validating our

426

paradigm. Second, in line with our expectation that context-dependent suppression of fear is similar

427

to extinction recall, we found increased ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC) responses to the

428

CS+ in the safe context compared to the CS+ in the threat context. Third, we found above-chance

429

decoding of environment-specific representations within the virtual environment (measured before

430

fear acquisition took place). Lastly, interindividual differences in context representation predicted

431

later differential fear conditioning strength, independent of the context. Critically, weaker context

432

representations prior to fear learning predicted later fear generalization.

433

We found increased SCRs as well as increased BOLD responses in regions typically activated

434

in response to cues associated with threat, in a safe context (i.e., where no aversive event has taken

435

place). Heightened fear responses in a safe environment is one of the hallmarks of fear and stress-

436

related disorders (Jovanovic, Kazama, Bachevalier, & Davis, 2012). Here, we found that such a

437

response to a potentially threatening cue in a safe context is present in a healthy population. We

438

extend previous findings of fear generalization that have shown generalized fear responses to

439

perceptually similar CSs (Dunsmoor, Mitroff, & LaBar, 2009; Lissek et al., 2008; Struyf, Zaman,

440

Hermans, & Vervliet, 2017), which were additionally shown to be accompanied by increased BOLD

441

responses in regions implicated in fear acquisition (Dunsmoor, Prince, Murty, Kragel, & LaBar, 2011).

442

We also extend a previous study that has shown fear responses to a CS associated with an aversive

443

event in a safe context (Baas et al., 2004), by showing these generalized physiological responses are

444

accompanied by increased activity in neural systems involved in fear learning.

445

In line with our hypothesis regarding the role of the vmPFC in context-dependent expression

446

of conditioned fear, we observed increased vmPFC responses to the CS+ in the safe context compared

447

to the CS+ in the threat context. The vmPFC is critically implicated in extinction of fear memory, as

448

detailed in animal models (Milad & Quirk, 2012). Human studies using neuroimaging have indicated

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/514505; this version posted January 9, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not
certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

449

that vmPFC responses increase over the course of extinction, in response a to CS+ that no longer

450

predicts an aversive outcome (Phelps et al., 2004). Increased vmPFC responses were also found in

451

response to an extinguished CS+ compared to a non-extinguished CS+, implicating a role of the

452

vmPFC in extinction recall (Milad et al. 2007). It is thought that extinction learning does not overwrite

453

the orginal fear memory (i.e., US-CS association), but creates a new safety memory that may be

454

context-dependent (Bouton 2004). It has been suggested that the extinction-induced vmPFC

455

responses following extinction are therefore also context-dependent (Kalish et al., 2006). The

456

increased vmPFC responses we found to a cue signaling threat in a safe context suggest that context-

457

dependent safety learning involves a similar neural pathway as extinction learning. We extend

458

previous findings, however, by showing that vmPFC responses do not per se progress over time but

459

can flexibly shift across a threat and safe context. Thus, our findings indicate that the role of the

460

vmPFC extends beyond one-directional extinction and suppression of fear expression, and is better

461

understood as implementing the capacity to flexibly regulate context-dependent expression of fear.

462

We found above-chance classification of environment-specific represantations within a

463

virtual reality environment from multi-voxel patterns of BOLD-fMRI recorded within the

464

hippocampus. This is in line with previous studies (Hassabis et al. 2009; Kim et al., 2017; Rodriguez,

465

2010; Sulpizio et al. 2014). One question that often arises is whether neuronal representations of the

466

spatial environment, which are known to be coded across distributed neuronal ensembles within the

467

hippocampus, can truly be picked up using a relatively coarse technique such as BOLD-fMRI. Indeed,

468

it is possible that environment-specific represantations could be decoded based on other features

469

than a purely spatial code (e.g., multimodal associations reinstated by exposure to a context).

470

Notably, however, it was recently found that in addition to sinlge-neuron firing rates, local-field

471

potentials (LFP) can also be used to decode spatial location in rodents, and this measure appears as

472

reliabe as neural spikes (Agarwal et al 2014). Because LFPs are also thought to underlie the BOLD

473

signal (Magri, Schridde, Murayama, Panzeri, & Logothetis, 2012), it appears reasonable to assume

474

that similar spatial representations could be detected using BOLD as well. Indeed, human

475

neuroimaging studies using BOLD-fMRI in humans have revealed that similar mechanisms are

476

involved in coding space and location as in rodents (Doeller, Barry, Burgess 2010). Namely, using

477

virtual reality paradigms, it was shown that BOLD signal in MTL regions follows a grid-like pattern

478

for space (Doeller, Barry, Burgess 2010), but also in relation to mental stimulation (Bellmund et al.,

479

2017) and visual space (Nau et al., 2018).

480

Our finding is in line with previous studies (Hassabis et al. 2009; Kim et al., 2017; Rodriguez,

481

2010; Sulpizio et al. 2014), however, there are a few differences between our design and those of

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/514505; this version posted January 9, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was not
certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

482

previous studies. First, in our design, participants were able to move freely within the virtual reality

483

environment, and within the context training paradigm, trials start at random locations within the

484

environment to promote path formation (Cei et al., 2014). Second, we have performed statistical

485

testing on z-scores, based on individual permutation distrubutions, and not the individual

486

classification accuracies, because this was shown to be more valid (Haynes, 2015). Third, in our

487

design, we fully controlled for visual confounds. The two buildings were fully identical on the inside,

488

and as input for the classifier we used a seven-second fixation period during which only a fixation

489

dot was visible on the screen.

490

Finally, we hypothesized that low context representations would be associated with more

491

fear generalization. We indeed found evidence for this association, however, we also found that

492

participants for whom the classifier was able to classify in which building they were in the virtual

493

environment during the training paradigm (i.e., before fear acquisition took place) had stronger

494

differential fear responses in the threat context across all learning trials. These two findings are

495

indications of how contextual information can play a dual role in fear acquisition. We propose that,

496

on the one hand, contextual information enhancee adaptive responses to threat by increasing its

497

specificity. Impaired discrimination between threat and safe cues has been linked to high anxiety

498

(Kuhn, Mertens, & Lonsdorf, 2016), suggesting that an increased cue discrimination reflects an

499

adaptive response to threat. Our finding suggests that context representation plays a role in this

500

discrimination. On the other hand, failure to integrate cue and context information into a conjunctive

501

memory may underlie the development of maladaptive threat responses that generalize to safe

502

environments.For instance, the inability to regulate fear using context information is thought to

503

underlie the development of post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD; Liberzon and Sripada, 2008;

504

Cohen et al., 2009; Acheson et al., 2011). Our findings provide empirical support for this notion by

505

linking poor spatial context representations to the inability to reguate fear responses in a safe

506

context.

507

In conclusion, we demonstrate generalized conditioned fear responses to a safe context

508

within a virtual reality environment. We found that the vmPFC is activated to threat cues in a safe

509

context, suggesting that this region uses contextual information to flexibly adjust expression of

510

conditioned fear, in a similar fashion as during recall of extinction memory. Furthermore, we

511

observed that good context representations are associated with stronger fear learning in the correct

512

context, while poor context representations were associated with stronger fear generalization (i.e.,

513

conditioned fear expression in a safe context). These experimental findings in a healthy population

515
