Abstract

11

Protein-protein interactions (PPI) can be conveniently represented as networks, allowing the

12

use of graph theory in their study. Network topology studies may reveal patterns associated to

13

specific organisms. Here we propose a new methodology to denoise PPI networks and predict

14

missing links solely based on the network topology, the Organization Measurement (OM)

15

method. The OM methodology was applied in the denoising of the PPI networks of two

16

Saccharomyces Cerevisiae datasets (Yeast and CS2007) and one Homo Sapiens dataset

17

(Human). To evaluate the denoising capabilities of OM methodology, two strategies were

18

applied. The first compared its application in random networks and in the reference set

19

networks, while the second perturbed the networks with the gradual random addition and

20

removal of edges. The application of OM methodology to the Yeast and Human reference sets

21

achieved an AUC of 0.95 and 0.87, in Yeast and Human networks, respectively. The random

22

removal of 80% of the Yeast and Human reference sets interactions resulted in an AUC of 0.71

23

and 0.62, whereas the random addition of 80% interactions resulted in an AUC of 0.75 and

24

0.72, respectively. Applying the OM methodology to the CS2007 dataset yields an AUC of

25

0.99. We also perturbed the network of the CS2007 dataset by randomly inserting and

26

removing edges in the same proportions previously described. The false positives identified

27

and removed from the network varied from 97%, when inserting 20% more edges, to 89%

28

when 80% more edges were inserted. The true positives identified and inserted in the network

29

varied from 95% when removing 20% of the edges, to 40% after the random deletion 80%

30

edges. The OM methodology is sensitive to the topological structure of the biological networks.

Introduction

34

Proteins are central players in every organism, as they are required for virtually every single

35

cellular function. However, proteins are required to interact with one another to fulfil their

36

functions. For this reason, disease states may appear, if the physiological interaction between

37

two proteins is disrupted [1].

38

Protein-protein interactions (PPI) networks are a subset of complex biological networks that

39

have specific topological properties, such as a high clustering coefficient, the presence of

40

hierarchy, heterogeneity and a power-law-like degree distribution [2]. The guilt-by-associa tio n

41

hypothesis states that two proteins sharing many interactive neighbours are likely to hold

42

functional homogeneity and localization coherence [3]. These characteristics suggest that

43

network topology alone may be a viable option for PPI network denoising. PPI network

44

denoising corresponds to find interactions that do not exist and to find missing interactio ns.

45

Methods to determine protein interactions are not accurate and organisms are not yet fully

46

known, being important to denoise PPI networks to have more precise models of the organis ms.

47

Protein interactions can be represented as graphs, allowing the use of graph theory in their

48

study. As such, different methods were developed to denoise biological networks [reviewed in

49

4]. These include repeating experiments [5, 6], using prior knowledge about proteins [7, 8],

50

using functional or structural annotations [9-13] and using comparisons with theoretica l

51

distributions constructed from known data and network topology-based approaches [14-18].

52

The herein proposed approach falls under the latter category.

53

An approach, called Non-Convex Semantic Embedding (NCSE) evaluates the reliability of

54

interactions in a PPI network trying to learn a Euclidean embedding under the geometric

55

assumption of PPI networks [19] and it was tested in three data sets of Yeast Saccharomyces

56

Cerevisiae. Computational methods based on machine learning were also applied to evaluate

57

the reliability of PPI and tested in Yeast and Helicobacter Pylori PPI datasets [20, 21].

58

In a recent study, LÃ¼ et al. [22] proposed the structural consistency index and the Structural

59

Perturbation Method (SPM). On the one hand, the structural consistency index can reflect the

60

inherent link predictability of a network without knowing its organization a priori, allowing to

61

estimate the explicability of the organization of a network, and to supervise mechanistic

62

changes during the evolution of the network. On the other hand, the SPM performs link

63

prediction by removing a percentage of the edges in a network, thus perturbing the remaining

64

network by that percentage. This is based on the strong correlation between independent

65

network perturbations, which suggests that the missing edges, i.e., False Negative (FN)

66

interactions can be identified by perturbing the networks with an additional set of known

67

interactions, i.e., True Positive (TP) interactions.

68

Luo et al. [4] proposed the Collaborative Filtering-enhanced Topology-based (CFT) method to

69

perform protein interatomic mapping on sparse High-Throughput Screening (HTS) PPI data,

70

since the performance of network topology-based approach usually deteriorates when using

71

sparse network data. This approach is based on the notion that the solution space of the

72

interatomic mapping and the solution space of the personalized recommendations are similar.

73

Each protein is represented as a feature vector that describes their interactions in the network.

74

In addition, the feature vector is used to calculate the corresponding similarity vector that

75

represents the interactions through the Functional Similarity weight, creating an Inter-

76

Neighbourhood Similarity (I-Sim) for modelling PPIs. Functional parameters for each protein

77

in the dataset are obtained from Gene Ontology (GO), allowing the use of functional similar ity

78

measures. Denoising of the input HTS-PPI data is performed via the integration of saturation-

79

based strategies into the I-Sim, achieving a precise relationship model. Their method was

80

applied to three different datasets and compared with three other algorithms (Interactio n

81

Generality [14], Czekanowski-Dice distance [15], and Functional Similarity weight [16]),

82

showing better performance on large, sparse HTS-PPI datasets. Since they use GO annotatio ns

83

to characterize their proteins, this approach is likely to underperform when considering less

84

studied organisms.

85

A different strategy termed Intrinsic Geometry Structure (IGS) was proposed by Yi Fang et al.

86

[23]. IGS is a geometry-based approach which uses heat diffusion in the PPI network to collect

87

structural information about all paths connecting two given nodes, thus defining intrins ic

88

relationships among them. They use a maximum likelihood-based algorithm to determine the

89

optimal dissipation time, predicting the global structure of the PPI network from the local

90

structure. After performing heat diffusion for the optimal dissipation time, the intrins ic

91

geometric structure of the PPI network is revealed. One of the main advantages of the IGS

92

method is its robustness against missing protein associations and sparse PPI data. Their method

93

was tested with the S. cerevisiae (CS2007) network [24], a network of the bottlenose dolphin

94

community [25], and a network of known terrorist cells [26]. In addition, they compared the

95

performance of IGS with two other methods, the Multi-Dimensional Scaling-based (MDS)

96

method [27] and the Hierarchical Random Graph (HRG) method [28], showing that IGS

97

performed slightly better than MDS when tested with the CS2007 dataset and better than IGS

and HRG for the other datasets tested. Their analysis was based only in the Area Under the

99

Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) Curve (AUC) values.

100

Among the described works, the MDS method proposed by Kuchaiev et al. [27] is the only one

101

relying in PPI network topology that was applied in a Homo Sapiens PPI dataset. To address

102

the sparsity problem of the networks, Luo et al. [4] uses collaborative filtering, but the method

103

was not tested on perturbed networks, i.e., when added random noise. The IGS method [23]

104

was compared to the MDS method using the Yeast Saccharomyces Cerevisiae PPI dataset, and

105

both methods were tested in perturbed networks with the same percentages (from 10% till

106

80%). However, they were not able to determine which of the edges recovered belong to

107

removed group, or which of the edges removed are then recovered.

108

In this paper, we introduce the Organization Measurement (OM) method to denoise PPI

109

networks based exclusively in the network topology. Topological measures are used to find

110

trends that characterize interacting and non-interacting proteins distributions. A high

111

confidence set of protein interactions is used to construct a network, followed by the calculatio n

112

of the weights of interactions and non-interactions in the network. The OM weighted matrix is

113

obtained and used to find distribution trends that allow to distinguish interaction distributio ns

114

from non-interaction distributions. The OM threshold value that better distinguishes these types

115

of distributions is then used to identify False Positive (FP) interactions and FN (novel)

116

interactions. This way, an OM topological model is built to be used in the denoising of a

117

network, resulting in a better approximation of the expected network.

Measures for similarity and diversity analysis of network data

184

Protein interactions can be conveniently modelled as a network, where each node represents a

185

protein and each edge represents an association between two proteins. The most commonly

186

used technique to quantify the interaction profile similarity of protein interaction networks (or

187

any type of biological network) relies on association indices. Bass et al. [33] performed a

188

comprehensive review on the selection of association indices for the analysis of gene similar ity.

189

In their work, the Jaccard (JC), Geometric and Cosine indices were shown to be the most

190

versatile, as though not excelling in any particular task, their strengths were the most balanced

191

out of all evaluated measures. A review of similarity indices can also be found in [34].

192

Daminelli et al. test the application of different association indexes to bipartite networks [35].

193

A more recent study reports that the JC measure performs better than three other measures in

194

a specific model [36].

Organization measurement threshold value determination

234

One of the assumptions made in this work is that the PPIs in the reference datasets are true.

235

This assumption can be made due to the sparsity of protein interaction networks and the

236

rigorous criteria chosen to filter TP interactions. However, the same cannot be said for the non-

237

interactions, as the presence of FN PPI is highly likely.

238

The value that best distinguishes both interactions and non-interactions distributions was called

239

the OM threshold value. First, we collect protein interactions data of a specific organism and

240

then a network is built (Fig 1). Then, the respective adjacency matrix is constructed, followed

241

by its transformation into a weighted matrix, the OM matrix, using the topological measures

242

of interest. Finally, the ROC curve is calculated and used to determine the optimal cut-off,

243

corresponding to the threshold value that separates the interaction distributions from non-

244

interaction distributions. We considered as the optimal cut, the point closest to (0,1) in the ROC

245

curve, where sensitivity equals specificity. Different topological measures were tested and the

246

respective cut-off values were determined. The outcomes of these experiments are described

247

in the â€œResults and discussionâ€ section.

Organization measurement methodology to find spurious and new interactions

249

An accepted assumption in network topology-based approaches is that interacting proteins in

250

a local community and closer to one another in the network are most likely involved in similar

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/527606; this version posted September 26, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which

251

functions, or part of the same pathways [39-41]. The use of topological measures that capture

252

this information should be prioritized, as they are expected to better grasp patterns in

253

incomplete networks, thus allowing the approximation of incomplete input networks to the real

254

networks.

255

From each reference set (Reference Set) PPI network we calculated its adjacency matrix. Then,

256

after calculating the respective weights, the adjacency matrix is transformed into a weight

257

matrix. Finally, the threshold that best separates PPI and non-PPIs was determined through the

258

finding of the optimal cut-off of the ROC curve. This threshold was applied to detect spurious

259

and missing PPI in the network (Data Set), to obtain a better approximation of the true network.

260

In the example network shown in Fig 1, there are five nodes representing five different proteins,

261

in addition to six edges that could represent the interactions between them (Data Set).

262

Assuming the example network approximates the current knowledge of a given biologic a l

263

network, not all true interactions are represented and the existence of FP is expected. Once the

264

threshold value is calculated, using the reference set (Reference Set), it is applied to the OM

265

matrix of the Data Set, to identify FP and FN interactions. FP interactions are then removed

266

from the network, whereas FN interactions are added.

422

Different topological measures were used to identify the optimal threshold, with the Yeast and

423

Human reference sets and comparative testing showed (Figs 2 and 3 and Table 2) that the best

424

results were obtained using the JC and the NC measures, and thus we decided to use both in

425

some experiments of this work. JC is a widely known measure frequently used in network

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/527606; this version posted September 26, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which
was not certified by peer review) is the author/funder. All rights reserved. No reuse allowed without permission.

426

denoising and missing link prediction. It also considers the neighbourhood information, which

427

is aligned with the â€œguilt-by-associationâ€ principle. The same applies to the NC index,

428

proposed herein, where the concept of CC is also taken into account.

429

The OM methodology was then applied to the Yeast and Human datasets, using the JC and the

430

NC measures and after analysing Tables 3 and 4, where the AUC values and the cut-off values

431

for both the optimal cut and the accuracy cut for the Yeast and Human reference sets obtained

432

are shown, it can be seen that the NC measure performed better than the JC measure at

433

discriminating between protein interactions and non-interactions and for this reason the NC

434

measure was used in the evaluation of the OM methodology.

435

Three different scenarios were considered to evaluate the OM methodology. The first one uses

436

randomly generated protein networks, with the same number of nodes and edges as their

437

respective reference sets (Yeast and Human). Observing the Fig 4 we can see that the AUC,

438

obtained when applying the OM methodology to one of the random networks, was close to 0.5

439

for both organisms (Yeast and Human), while for the Yeast and Human reference sets, the

440

AUC was 0.9534 and 0.8708, respectively (Fig 2), which shows that OM is sensitive to the

441

inherent topological structure of a real network. These results show that the OM methodology

442

cannot distinguish between interactions and non-interactions in random networks, but can

443

capture the inherent rules of biological networks, not present in random networks.

444

The second scenario used to evaluate the performance of OM methodology, consisted in

445

applying OM to networks obtained from the two Yeast and Human reference sets, where the

446

number of nodes (proteins) was maintained, but where a random percentage of edges (proteins

447

interactions), 20%, 40%, 60% and 80%, not belonging to the reference set network, were

448

added, and the third scenario is similar to the second but instead of adding, the same random

449

percentages of edges were removed from the reference set network.

450

In the second scenario (random insertion of edges), as expected, greater increments of random

451

edges resulted in greater performance reductions (Fig 5). The performance reductions were

452

steeper in Human, which could be attributed to one major reason: the percentage of FN is most

453

likely greater in the Human interactome than in the Yeast interactome. Thus, it could be argued

454

that the Yeast reference set is a more reliable, better representation of the actual Yeast

455

interactome, than the Human reference set is of the real Human interactome. When we add

456

these percentages of random edges, the inherent structure of these biological networks becomes

457

deteriorated, because we are probably adding TN.

bioRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/527606; this version posted September 26, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which

458

In the third scenario (random deletion of edges), greater reductions in the number of edges

459

result in greater performance drops compared to the second scenario, but the performance drops

460

are steeper in the Human organism (Fig 7). This could be explained by the fact that we are

461

removing TP from both networks. However, since the Yeast network seems to be a closer

462

representation of its true network than the Human network, the accentuated deterioration in the

463

structure of the Human network could explain this behaviour.

464

So, when comparing the results between edge addition and edge removal in Yeast and Human

465

reference sets (Figs 5 and 7), we witness that the overall performance reductions were quite

466

dissimilar. Adding just 20% more edges contributed to a reduction of approximately 0.08 in

467

AUC for Yeast, and 0.06 AUC for Human. Further addition of edges beyond this point did not

468

decrease the AUC as sharply. Contrariwise, after removing 20% of the existing edges, the AUC

469

decreased by roughly 0.02 for both Yeast and Human, with greater performance drops afte r

470

each percentage of edges removal.

471

The better performance observed for the Yeast interactome could be explained by its smaller

472

size compared to the Human interactome, in addition to being relatively well-studied, meaning

473

that input data quality plays an important role in the performance of computational methods.

474

Additionally, the negative impact in performance observed after randomly adding edges

475

suggests that the OM methodology is very sensitive to high percentages of FP and FN.

476

To compare the performance of OM methodology with other network-based methodologies

477

proposed by other researchers, CS2007 network reference set was perturbed by randomly

478

inserting edges in the same proportions previously described in scenario two and by randomly

479

deleting edges in the same proportions previously described in scenario three. OM

480

methodology was compared to the MDS and IGS methodologies [23]. Figs 6 and 8 show the

481

AUC values when these methodologies were applied to this dataset and it can be seen a general

482

improvement in the performance when the OM methodology is applied compared to the MDS

483

and IGS methodologies. When 80% of random TP of network interactions are removed, the

484

AUC of IGS is superior than the AUC od OM and MDS. This could not mean, in this case, that

485

OM is worst, since removing 80% of the TP makes the model very close to a random network

486

and a denoising method, for consistency, should not be able to detect structure in such

487

networks.

489

interactions. OM methodology was applied and the percentage of FP interactions removed was

490

calculated (Table 5). Interestingly, most of the randomly inserted FP interactions were

promptly identified, even when the network was heavily perturbed, with 89% of the FP

493

that the OM methodology can indeed capture the inherent topology of biological networks.

Interestingly, we observed that the number of TP identified after randomly removing edges

500

501

meaning that the percentage of FP and FN cannot hide the inherent structure behind the

502

biological networks of the organisms.

503

Conclusions

504

Currently, low-throughput experimental methods are the only effective way to validate protein

505

interactions. While high-throughput experimental methods to obtain PPIs exist, the obtained

506

results have very high noise. As such, computational methods are required to speed data

507

acquisition and to reduce the data contamination. Methods relying exclusively in the topology

508

of biological networks are simpler and faster, as it appears that networks topology may reveal

509

patterns or signatures associated with the kind of organism and the type of interactions. If we

510

can use, effectively, only the topology to denoise biological networks, we have a simple

511

computational method suitable for incomplete interactomes, without the need of extra

512

biological knowledge.

513

This paper introduces the OM methodology for denoising biological networks, a methodology

514

that: a) uses exclusively the topology of the network; b) enables, easily, to separate the

515

distributions of interaction and non-interaction proteins in PPI networks; c) does not use known

516

distributions as approximations; and d) provides a topological way of detecting FP interactio ns

517

and find new interactions. The main innovation of the OM methodology is related with its

518

ability to combine the advantages of using exclusively the topology without taking

519

approximations to known distributions and without using external knowledge to detect

520

interactions that do not exist or to find new interactions with a better performance than some

521

documented used methodologies. This paper also introduces a new network topologic measure,

522

the NC measure, which is used with the OM methodology and yielded better results, compared

523

to other known and current topological measures.
