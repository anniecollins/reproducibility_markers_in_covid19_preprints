Abstract

2

There has been a growing interest in the autonomous sensory meridian response (ASMR). The

3

ASMR is characterized by a tingling sensation around the scalp and neck and often induces a feeling

4

of relaxation and a reduction of a negative mood. However, it is still unknown what factors affect

5

the ASMR. The present study focused on stimulus characteristics and individuals‚Äô mood states and

6

personality traits. Participants filled out self‚Äêreported questionnaires (the Profile of Mood States,

7

Beck Depression Inventory, and Big Five Inventory) and reported ASMR estimates throughout a 17‚Äê

8

min experiment while listening to binaural tapping and brushing sounds. Cross‚Äêcorrelation results

9

showed that the ASMR estimates were strongly associated with the acoustic features of auditory

10

stimuli, such as their amplitude, spectral centroid, and spectral bandwidth. This indicates that low‚Äê

11

pitched sounds with dark timbre trigger the ASMR. The maximum ASMR was observed around 2 s

12

after the acoustic features changed, suggesting that the sluggishness of multisensory integration

13

may lead to the ASMR experience. In addition, individual differences in the ASMR experience were

14

closely linked to participants‚Äô mood states, such as anxiety, but not to their personality traits. Our

15

results provide important clues to understand the mechanisms of auditory‚Äêsomatosensory

16

interactions.

Introduction

2

Many regular‚Äêto‚Äêheavy users of the internet are familiar with the autonomous sensory

3

meridian response (ASMR), a phenomenon often characterized by a tingling, electrostatic‚Äêlike

4

sensation across the scalp and back of the neck (Barratt and Davis, 2015). As the ASMR can be

5

triggered by a variety of audiovisual stimuli, content creators have produced hundreds of ASMR‚Äê

6

inducing videos for video‚Äêsharing platforms such as YouTube. Many individuals experience ASMR to

7

receive a feeling of relaxation, reduce a negative mood, and aid sleep (Barratt and Davis, 2015).

8

In contrast to the general population‚Äôs interest in ASMR, few scientific studies sought to

9

clarify the types of stimuli that most effectively trigger the ASMR and how the ASMR is related to

10

an individual‚Äôs mental states and personality traits. Recent studies have used web‚Äêbased surveys of

11

the Big Five Inventory (BFI) and demonstrated that individuals with higher Openness and lower

12

Conscientiousness scores reported more frequent ASMR experiences (McErlean and Banissy, 2017;

13

Fredborg et al., 2018). However, such studies include an inherent sample selection bias in that

14

participants recruited through the internet may have the tendency to disclose their ASMR

15

experiences. In addition, how the stimulus characteristics were controlled is unclear, which may

16

inherently affect the ASMR.

17

From the perspective of cross‚Äêmodal interactions, the ASMR is an intriguing phenomenon

18

because sounds induce a shiver or thrill that corresponds to a tickling sensation on the skin (Grewe

19

et al., 2009). There is no doubt that the integration of vision, touch, and proprioception plays a

20

critical role in shaping the body schema (Maravita et al., 2003; de Vignemont et al., 2005; Haggard

21

et al., 2007). However, few studies have looked at the contribution of audition to the

22

somatosensory system. Effects of auditory distractors on tactile discrimination are larger when the

23

distractors are presented close to the head (20 cm) than when they are presented far from the

24

head (70 cm) (Kitagawa et al., 2005). Self‚Äêproduced action sounds bias the body schema and its

25

spatial boundaries (Tajadura‚ÄêJimenez et al., 2012). In addition, auditory feedback can modify the

26

tactile sensation of a listener experiences when rubbing his/her hands together (Jousm√§ki and Hari,

1998; Guest et al., 2002). When high‚Äêfrequency components of the auditory feedback increase, the

2

3

Actually, listeners reported a tickling sensation when listening to sounds while the ear of a dummy

4

head was stroked with a brush (Kitagawa and Igarashi, 2005). However, it is unclear what factors

5

transform auditory inputs into the tickling sensation.

6

The present study examined the degree to which the ASMR is affected by the acoustic

7

features of auditory stimuli, as well as by mood states and personality traits of individuals.

8

Participants, all of whom had never experienced the ASMR, were instructed to listen to binaural

9

tapping and brushing sounds from headphones. We hypothesized that there should be no

10

confounding factor between acoustic features and an individual‚Äôs states/traits because these

11

parameters are essentially independent of each other. The ASMR may be similar to aesthetic chills

12

in terms of a sound‚Äêinduced emotional experience. After the experiment, we interviewed listeners

13

to investigate the pleasantness of the ASMR experience.

15

16

17

18

19

21

22

23

24

25

Discussion
The present results demonstrate that the intensity of ASMR estimates varied across

19

listeners, although its sensation mainly originated on their ears, neck, and shoulders. The ASMR

20

was closely linked to the tickling sensation but not to the pleasant‚Äêunpleasant dimension. The

21

ASMR was affected by the acoustic features of auditory stimuli. Specifically, the cross‚Äêcorrelation

22

results showed that the ASMR was induced by interaural differences in the acoustic features, such

23

as the amplitude, spectral centroid, and spectral bandwidth. The maximum ASMR was observed

24

around 2 s after the acoustic features changed. In addition, it was associated with listener‚Äôs mood

25

states, including anxiety. In what follows, we mainly discuss effects of stimulus characteristics on

26

the ASMR.

Many internet users expect the ASMR to provide a feeling of relaxation (Barratt and Davis,

2

2015). Our preliminary study using a test‚Äêretest paradigm indicated that the ASMR itself has high

3

reliability within ASMR‚Äêinexperienced participants (Supplementary Fig. 2). It seems that the ASMR

4

differs from music‚Äêinduced chills in terms of physiological, emotional, and phenomenological

5

aspects. Piloerection during chills is innervated by the sympathetic nervous system (Lindsley and

6

Sassaman, 1938; Craig, 2005). There is no consensus that the parasympathetic nervous system is

7

involved in the ASMR (Poerio et al., 2018). A profound emotional experience, such as weeping,

8

sometimes arises in response to music‚Äêinduced chills (Sloboda, 1991; Panksepp, 1995; Mori and

9

Iwanaga, 2017), but not in response to the ASMR. In contrast to aesthetic chills, the ASMR is

10

frequently triggered by a variety of stimuli, such as whispering, close personal attention, and crisp

11

sounds (Barratt and Davis, 2015). The present study investigated common factors (i.e., acoustic

12

features) affecting various ASMR stimuli.

13

We have demonstrated that the Diff‚Äêfeatures of auditory stimuli play an important role in

14

triggering the ASMR. Specifically, a larger Diff‚Äêamplitude parameter resulted in larger ASMR

15

estimates. The Diff‚Äêamplitude used in the present study corresponds to an interaural level

16

difference (ILD). Thus, the results can be interpreted as indicating that the ASMR is elicited when

17

listeners perceive sound sources near their ears. This is consistent with the findings that the ASMR

18

is longer during binaural listening than during diotic listening (Liao et al., 2017) and stronger for

19

sounds moving around the head than for static sounds (Honda et al., 2019). In a previous study,

20

listeners reported a stronger tickling sensation when the sound of an ear being tickled was

21

presented with headphones than when the sound was presented via a loudspeaker placed 80 cm

22

from their ears (Kitagawa and Igarashi, 2005). Therefore, it is likely that auditory‚Äìsomatosensory

23

interactions observed as the ASMR are related to sound localization ability in the space close to the

24

head.

26

Most sound localization studies have focused on auditory events in the distal region (i.e.,
distances of more than 1 m from listeners) (Brungart and Rabinowitz, 1999). This may be because

the detection of distant events in an environment is believed to be necessary for the survival of

2

humans and mammals (Heffner and Heffner, 1992; Kitagawa and Spence, 2006). In general, the ILD

3

and interaural time difference (ITD) are used as localization cues, respectively, when high‚Äê and low‚Äê

4

frequency sounds reach the ears (Rayleigh, 1907; Moore, 2013). ILDs can be accurately obtained at

5

high frequencies (more than 1.5 kHz) because diffraction due to the head hardly occurs, whereas

6

ITDs can be easily detected at low frequencies because phase ambiguity hardly occurs. Intriguingly,

7

a previous study demonstrated that ILDs, even at low frequencies, increase when a sound source is

8

located less than 50 cm from the head (Brungart and Rabinowitz, 1999).Thus, we believe that

9

listeners in our experiment used ILDs, as well as ITDs, to estimate the distance and direction of a

10

sound source, which results in the enhancement of sensitivity to the ASMR.

11

Our results showed that the Ave‚Äêcentroid feature was negatively correlated with the ASMR

12

estimate. The spectral centroid has been shown to be correlated with the perceptual brightness of

13

musical sounds with clear pitch (Almeida et al., 2017). Although our stimuli were non‚Äêmusical

14

natural sounds, a previous study revealed that both pitched and unpitched sounds induce timbre in

15

a common perceptual space, which includes the spectral centroid as one of its major dimensions

16

(Lakatos, 2000). Thus, it is likely that sounds with a dark impression are closely linked to the ASMR

17

experience.

18

In contrast, the Ave‚Äê and Diff‚Äêroughness had a limited effect on the ASMR. It is known that

19

roughness in sounds such as screams induces unpleasant perception (Arnal et al., 2015). The

20

perceptual attribute can be differentiated from other communication signals such as speech. In this

21

study, the cross‚Äêcorrelation between ASMR estimates and Ave‚Äêroughness was not significant but

22

was positive. This is consistent with the behavioral results indicating that the ASMR is not

23

necessarily a pleasant experience for some listeners. Alternatively, listeners might experience an

24

unpleasant feeling because the auditory stimuli were not accompanied with visual information.

26

It may be hypothesized that a sluggish integration of multisensory information, in particular

5

10

13

14

The auditory and somatosensory systems have similar temporal frequency channels (Yau et

16

al., 2009; Occelli et al., 2011). Many neuropsychological studies have investigated somatosensory

17

18

19

et al., 2001; Fu et al., 2003). Tactile stimulation has been shown to enhance the activity of posterior

20

21

known about how neurons in the cerebral cortex encode information on auditory distance. A

22

23

to auditory stimuli presented within 30 cm from the head (Graziano et al., 1999) and that neural

24

responses decrease when they are presented at distances of 50 cm from the head. The traditional

25

view on sensory systems is that different sensory information is separately analyzed in unisensory

26

areas and subsequently projected into multisensory areas (Felleman and Van Essen, 1991).

12

13

ASMR. An essential aspect of the ASMR is that auditory stimuli produce different forms of

14

15

induced by changes in interaural differences in the acoustic features, namely the amplitude,

16

17

18

19
