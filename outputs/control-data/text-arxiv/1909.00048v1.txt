Abstract. Let Œ∫ ‚â§ 0, and let X be a locally-finite CAT(Œ∫) polyhedral 2complex X, each face with constant curvature Œ∫. Let E be a closed, rectifiablyconnected subset of X with trivial first singular homology. We show that E,

CAT(Œ∫) spaces are a well-studied generalization of nonpositive curvature from
Riemannian manifolds to metric spaces. Many combinatorial constructions, such
as MŒ∫ -polyhedral complexes, involve gluing together pieces of well-understood
CAT(Œ∫) spaces to form new ones. We extend the class of known examples by taking closed subsets of 2-dimensional polyhedral CAT(Œ∫) spaces and considering the
induced path metric; under proper assumptions, these subspaces are also CAT(Œ∫).
In [3], we showed that any closed, simply-connected, rectifiably-connected subset
of the plane (with a constant curvature Œ∫ ‚â§ 0 metric) is a complete CAT(Œ∫) space
when endowed with the induced path metric. In this paper, we extend this result
to similar subspaces of a CAT(Œ∫) polyhedral 2-complex. In particular, we prove
the following result.
Theorem A (Theorem 6.1). Let E be a closed, rectifiably-connected subspace of a
locally-finite CAT(Œ∫) MŒ∫ -polyhedral 2-complex X, where Œ∫ ‚â§ 0. If H1 (E) = 0 then
E, under the induced path metric, is a complete CAT(Œ∫) space.
(Throughout this paper, we assume Œ∫ ‚â§ 0.)
We remark that another potential route to prove Theorem A is to solve the
isoperimetric inequality for simple closed curves [2]. Unfortunately, it is not clear
how to do this; in particular, the behavior of simple closed curves can be rather
subtle in a CAT(Œ∫) 2-complex (see Example 1.4 below, for instance), and there is
no general 1-Lipschitz retraction from the full 2-complex to a closed subset, even if
the subset is contractible.
The paper proceeds as follows: We first generalize some aspects of the Jordan
Curve Theorem to the context of CAT(Œ∫) 2-complexes (Theorem 2.8). This allows
us to make assertions about the behavior of curves in Y at the points where they
are locally geodesic (Lemma 3.4). Finally, we reduce the theorem to the case where
X is planar, and use a characterization of angles proved in [3, Theorem B].
