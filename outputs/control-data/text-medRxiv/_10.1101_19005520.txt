Abstract

20

We proposed and tested a feminist social-ecological theory about daughters‚Äô experience of

21

female genital mutilation/cutting (FGMC) in Egypt, where over 90% of women ages 15‚Äì49 are cut.

22

FGMC has potential adverse effects on demographic and health outcomes and has been defined as a

23

human-rights violation. Contextual factors are important determinants of FGMC, but an integrated

24

theory is lacking, and quantitative multilevel research is limited. We theorized that more favorable

25

community-level gender systems, including gender norms opposing FGMC and expanded

26

opportunities for women outside of the family, would be associated with a daughter‚Äôs lower risk of

27

FGMC and would strengthen the negative association of a mother‚Äôs opposition to FGMC with her

28

daughter‚Äôs risk of cutting. Using a national sample of 14,171 mother-daughter dyads from the 2014

29

Egypt Demographic and Health Survey, we estimated multilevel discrete-time hazard models to test

30

these relationships. Community gender norms opposing FGMC had significant direct, negative

31

associations with the hazard that a daughter was cut, but women‚Äôs opportunities outside the family

32

did not. Maternal opposition to FGMC was negatively associated with cutting a daughter, and these

33

associations were stronger where community opposition to FGMC and opportunities for women

34

were greater. Results provided good support for a gender-systems theory of the multilevel influences

35

on FGMC. Integrated, multilevel interventions that address gender norms about FGMC and

36

opportunities for women in the community, as well as beliefs about the practice among the mothers

37

of at-risk daughters may be needed for sustainable declines in the practice.

Introduction

39

Female genital mutilation/cutting (FGMC) refers to ‚Äúprocedures involving the partial or total removal of

40

the external female genitalia or other injury to the female genital organs for non-medical reasons‚Äù

41

(1). The practice‚Äîespecially its more extensive forms‚Äìis associated with various health and

42

demographic outcomes, including elevated risks of an earlier age at first sex (2), sexual trauma and

43

dysfunction (3, 4), primary infertility (5), obstetric complications (6, 7), and marital dissatisfaction,

44

instability, and dissolution (2, 6, 8). The United Nations recognizes FGMC as a violation of human

45

rights (1), and Sustainable Development Goal 5.3 has established a global mandate to encourage

46

abandonment (9). Alongside legal and programmatic efforts to end FGMC (10), its prevalence has

47

declined in some places, remains common in at least 29 mostly African countries, and remains nearly

48

universal in eight countries, including Egypt (11, 12).

49

Community context influences various outcomes in childhood and adolescence related to

50

health (13, 14), sexual behavior (15), and early marriage (16). Research on FGMC has found that

51

community characteristics are associated with the practice among girls (17, 18), and most of this

52

research has focused on the normative context specific to FGMC. Yet, norms about FGMC are only

53

one aspect of a community gender system that includes structural constraints on women‚Äôs

54

opportunities outside of the family.

55

We advance research on FGMC in two ways. First, we offer an integrated explanatory

56

theory, focusing on two elements of a community-level gender system: the collective practice of FGMC as

57

a gender norm that upholds patriarchal family systems, and the structural opportunities for women outside

58

this family system. This framing highlights the salience of gender systems as an explanatory theory

59

of demographic (19-22) and life course (23, 24) processes. Defining and testing these elements of a

60

gender system also addresses debates about normative versus structural drivers of demographic and

61

behavioral change (25, 26), especially for gender-related outcomes, such as skewed sex ratios at birth,

Our study site is Egypt, where FGMC remains widespread despite legal prohibitions of the

67

practice. The 2014 Egypt Demographic and Health Survey (EDHS) provides rich data on FGMC

68

for all women respondents and their daughters (12, 27). This study fills conceptual and empirical

69

gaps in debates on the community influences on FGMC in what scholars have called the classic

70

patriarchal belt of the Middle East and North Africa (MENA) region. Our framework and findings

71

offer insights for multifaceted community interventions to further the abandonment of FGMC, and

72

other practices harmful to women and girls.

Background

74

Prevalence, trends, and determinants of FGMC

75

An estimated 200 million women and girls in 30 countries have experienced FGMC (28), although

76

estimates for the number affected vary (29). On the African continent, FGMC is practiced in at least

77

29 countries, with prevalence ranging from 1% in Cameroon and Uganda to 98% in Somalia (29).

78

Across 87 national surveys for 29 African countries, the prevalence of FGMC has declined

79

somewhat in more than half the countries; however, declines have been more pronounced in

80

countries with low-to-moderate prevalence than in countries with near-universal prevalence, such as

81

Egypt (11, 12, 29, 30).

82

Studies of the determinants of FGMC have focused on family or household

83

characteristics‚Äîa girl‚Äôs immediate social context. In diverse settings, the daughters of mothers with

84

more schooling (31, 32) and living in wealthier households (11, 33) less often have been cut. Other

salient social contexts include religious and ethnic identities, social networks among women, and

86

marriage markets (17, 18, 34-36).

87

Few studies have examined the community-level sources of variation in FGMC. Yet, even

88

neighboring villages can harbor different attitudes and practices related to FGMC, suggesting an

89

influence of community context on girls‚Äô experiences (37). In the first multilevel analyses of FGMC,

90

the community prevalence of FGMC was directly, positively associated with a girl‚Äôs risk of cutting in

91

Kenya (17) and modified the individual-level association of religious identity on this risk in Burkina

92

Faso (18). FGMC tends to be less common in urban than rural areas (11); however, such differences

93

may be reduced or reversed after accounting for other factors (17, 18, 32). The influences of gender-

94

related community characteristics are undertheorized and understudied.

Community gender systems and FGMC: Integrating social norms

96

and feminist theories

97

We propose an integrated, feminist, social-ecological framework, in which a community-level gender

98

system is our explanatory model (Fig 1). We describe how two elements of this gender system‚Äîgender

99

norms about FGMC and extra-familial opportunities for women‚Äîmay influence a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC,

100

directly and by conditioning the influence of maternal beliefs about and experience with the practice.

Community gender norms about FGMC

104

A social norm is a collective practice in a referent group that is motivated by two social expectations in

105

the group: the empirical expectation that others practice the behavior, and the normative expectation that

106

others think the practice should be upheld (38-41). A social norm is maintained through social

107

influence, or expectations that compliance will be rewarded with social acceptance, and non5

108

compliance will be punished with social rejection (41). Thus, complying with a social norm can be a

109

marker of social belonging (42-44), and sustaining the norm is a way to uphold these social ties as

110

distinct and valued (36).

111

In the case of FGMC, social norms theorists argue that the decision to cut or not to cut a

112

daughter will depend on parental perceptions of the collective practice and of the community‚Äôs

113

social beliefs (42, 43). A mother (usually the parent most directly involved in decisions about

114

FGMC) may personally believe that FGMC is unnecessary or even harmful, but she may have her

115

daughter cut if she perceives that: FGMC is practiced in her community, others think it should be

116

practiced, and non-compliance will have negative social consequences for her daughter. That said,

117

this mother may not have her daughter cut if she believes that her uncut daughter will be socially

118

accepted. Social norms approaches, therefore, imply an interaction between personal beliefs and

119

community characteristics. In communities where normative expectations are strongly enforced,

120

opposing personal beliefs will have little influence on behavior. As normative expectations weaken, a

121

‚Äútipping point‚Äù will be reached where the influence of personal opposition to FGMC can be

122

observed (42).

123

Social norms approaches to FGMC have some empirical support. The community-level

124

prevalence of FGMC, a proxy for empirical expectations about the practice, has been positively

125

associated with the risk of cutting a daughter in parts of Africa (17, 18). In communities in Egypt

126

and parts of Africa, public denunciations of FGMC have signaled changes in normative expectations

127

about the practice and have been thought to spur declines in prevalence (11, 45, 46). Such evidence

128

suggests that community-level empirical and normative expectations about FGMC matter; however,

129

the joint influences of these expectations are understudied, including whether a tipping point in

130

community opposition to FGMC is needed for maternal opposition to have an influence on a

131

daughter‚Äôs risk of cutting (47, 48).

A limitation of social norms theory is its cursory discussion of FGMC as integral to norms

133

of womanhood. In many practicing societies, FGMC is performed to achieve normative

134

expectations about sex differentiation, feminine beauty, and women‚Äôs sexuality (3, 8, 49, 50). First,

135

women achieve social recognition by becoming less like men physically (49); thus, by removing the

136

‚Äúmale parts‚Äù and sometimes enclosing the ‚Äúfemale parts,‚Äù FGMC ‚Äúfeminizes‚Äù a girl‚Äôs body (49-51).

137

Second, FGMC helps to realize the feminine aesthetics of purity, smoothness, and cleanliness (49,

138

51). Third, by de-emphasizing (or controlling) women‚Äôs sexuality, FGMC accentuates women‚Äôs

139

reproductive role (49). In these ways, the collective practice of FGMC acts as a gender norm, involving

140

empirical expectations that most women are cut, normative expectations that good women are cut, and

141

enforcement by requiring it for marriage in a patriarchal family system.

142

Community extra-familial opportunities for women

143

The gender norm of FGMC also may persist alongside institutionalized gender inequalities that limit

144

women‚Äôs opportunities outside the patriarchal family (31, 52-54). These structural inequalities are

145

multifaceted, arising in social, economic, and political domains (55). Women‚Äôs social inequality

146

involves a lack of access to education and the means to act on their sexual and reproductive choices.

147

Women‚Äôs economic inequality involves a lack of access to activities and institutions involving the

148

production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services (56). Together, these

149

institutionalized gender inequalities limit women‚Äôs access to social and economic resources and

150

identities outside of the family, cementing women‚Äôs dependence on the security provided by (male)

151

family members and conformity to practices that uphold this system. The strength of

152

institutionalized inequality can be measured by the availability of extra-familial opportunities for women,

153

that is, by women‚Äôs access to social and economic resources outside of the family. In lower- and

154

middle-income settings, evidence of extra-familial opportunities for women may include higher

155

average schooling attainments, later average ages at first marriage, lower rates of arranged marriage,

The availability of opportunities like these at the community level may allow mothers to see

158

real alternatives to having their daughters cut (31, 39, 57). Thus, a direct association may exist

159

between community extra-familial opportunities and a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC. Social norms

160

theorists have questioned the influence of women‚Äôs extra-familial opportunities on the risk of

161

FGMC, citing patriarchal settings where FGMC is not practiced and women‚Äôs involvement in its

162

continuation (58). These arguments ignore feminist theory (59). First, different forms of patriarchy

163

exist and are upheld by different gender norms, which may or may not include FGMC. Second,

164

women‚Äôs complicity in upholding harmful practices may reflect a survival strategy under patriarchy

165

(59), where mothers pursue the best available ‚Äúoptions‚Äù for their daughters in the absence of real

166

alternatives. To the extent that greater extra-familial opportunities at the community level signal a

167

broader system of alternatives for women, they may reduce the perceived costs of violating norms

168

favoring FGMC. Thus, an interaction also may exist between personal beliefs and community

169

context, whereby mothers feel able not to have their daughters cut in contexts where women have

170

sources of social and economic security outside of marriage and the marital family.

171

The Egyptian context

172

In Egypt, FGMC remains nearly universal and a majority of women favor continuation. In 1995,

173

97% of ever-married women 15‚Äì49 years were cut, and the median age at cutting was 10 (60-63).

174

Since the criminalization of FGMC in 2008, the adjusted prevalence of FGMC in girls 15‚Äì19 years

175

declined little, from 94% in 2008 to 88% in 2014 (64). More than half of ever-married women 15‚Äì49

176

years supported continuation in 2008 (62%) and 2014 (58%) (64).

177

Here, FGMC is closely tied to expectations about gender identification, feminine beauty and

178

purity, and control of women‚Äôs sexuality (32, 65-68). In Egyptian social systems, like many African

179

social systems, family and community are core institutions whose interests override those of an

181

183

185

In recent decades, minority Christian groups have begun to abandon FGMC while Islamist

186

groups have promoted FGMC as part of authentic Muslim womanhood (36). Thus, higher

187

percentages of Muslim than Christian women practice FGMC, intend to cut their daughters, and

188

189

190

191

views about the practice (69). Thus, the emergence of FGMC as a symbol of authentic Muslim

192

womanhood has transformed the practice from a widespread gender norm to a gender norm aligned

193

more narrowly with Muslim identity (36).

In this context, opportunities for women outside the family remain limited. In 1981, Egypt

195

ratified the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women

196

197

198

199

200

201

202

203

Summary and hypotheses

209

In sum, an integrated feminist social-ecological theory situates the decision to cut or not to cut a

210

daughter within a community gender system. This system includes gender norms that uphold or challenge

211

patriarchal marriage systems and extra-familial opportunities available or not available to women. Both

212

elements of this gender system will directly influence a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC and will condition

213

the influence of a mother‚Äôs opposition to FGMC on the risk that her daughter is cut. Specifically:

214

H1.

A daughter will have a lower adjusted hazard of FGMC in communities (a) that have a lower

215

prevalence of FGMC and more strongly oppose the practice, and (b) where women have more

216

extra-familial opportunities.

217

H2. A mother‚Äôs opposition to FGMC and non-cut status will be associated with a lower hazard of

218

her daughter being cut in communities (a) that have a lower prevalence of FGMC and more

219

strongly oppose the practice, and (b) where women have more extra-familial opportunities. In

220

communities with less favorable gender systems, maternal opposition to FGMC will not be

221

associated with a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC.

258

259

260

261

Discussion

484

This analysis was the first to test an integrated gender-systems theory for the community-

485

level influences on the hazard that a daughter experiences FGMC in a North African country that

486

historically has adhered to a classic patriarchal family system (59). We examined to what extent

487

norms opposing FGMC and more extra-familial opportunities for women in the community were

488

associated with a girl‚Äôs lower risk of FGMC. We also assessed to what extent maternal opposition to

489

FGMC was more strongly associated with the hazard of cutting a daughter in communities with

490

more unfavorable FGMC norms and greater extra-familial opportunities for women. We leveraged

491

rich data on FGMC from the 2014 Egypt DHS, which is well suited for multilevel analyses of

492

community characteristics.

493

As expected, more unfavorable FGMC norms in the community were directly associated

494

with a lower hazard that a daughter was cut (H1). However, extra-familial opportunities for women

495

in the community were not directly associated with this hazard. Still, as expected, maternal

496

opposition to FGMC was more negatively associated with the hazard that a daughter was cut in

497

communities with more unfavorable FGMC norms and greater extra-familial opportunities for

498

women (H2). These findings were robust to the inclusion of controls, and therefore, provide good

499

support for a gender-systems theory of the multilevel influences on FGMC. Thus, mothers who

500

oppose FGMC are less likely to have their daughter cut if they live in a community that more

501

strongly opposes FGMC and offers more opportunity for women outside the family.

502

Results were mixed about there being a ‚Äútipping point‚Äù in community FGMC norms and

503

women‚Äôs opportunities above which maternal opposition to FGMC reduces a daughter‚Äôs hazard of
24

medRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/19005520; this version posted October 18, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was
not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted medRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity.
It is made available under a CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license .

504

FGMC. In most age risk sets, the coefficient for the relationship of maternal opposition to FGMC

505

with a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC was significant and negative, even in communities that strongly

506

favored FGMC and had limited opportunities for women, relative to the average community in

507

Egypt. Evidence supporting a ‚Äútipping point‚Äù was strongest only for the youngest age risk set (0‚Äì1),

508

in which the probability of FGMC was very low.

509

Egypt provides a salient test case for our theory, being the country with the largest number

510

of girls and women who have been cut and a persistently high prevalence of FGMC (11). In

511

countries where more variation in the practice of FGMC exists, and thus a wider range of

512

community normative contexts, we might expect an even stronger association of an unfavorable

513

community gender system with daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC. Yet, some distinctive features of the

514

Egyptian context may make it difficult to generalize results across all countries where FGMC is

515

practiced. Egypt has a strong national identity dominated by a single ethnic group and a single

516

religion, although with visible minority groups (36). Thus, the nature of social identity and the

517

relationship of FGMC to a sense of group belonging may differ in Egypt than in the multiethnic

518

states that dominate sub-Saharan Africa. Moreover, although there is a large rural population in

519

Egypt, the Egyptian economy is more diversified and less dependent on subsistence agriculture than

520

are the economies of other countries where FGMC is common. The greater economic opportunity

521

outside family-based agriculture, coupled with persistent elements of classic patriarchy that reinforce

522

women‚Äôs economic dependence on male family members (59), may make expanded opportunities

523

for women outside the family more consequential in Egypt than in other contexts (34). Comparative

524

research to address these questions is warranted. Also, conditions surrounding the practice of

525

FGMC in diaspora communities, where the maintenance of gender norms is tied to questions of

526

cultural preservation and immigrant integration, may be distinct and require specific theorization.

527

Limitations and implications for research
25

medRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/19005520; this version posted October 18, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was
not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted medRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity.
It is made available under a CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license .

528

This analysis was not without limitations, which can inform recommendations for research.

529

First, the cross-sectional design of the DHS limited our ability to establish the timing of community-

530

level characteristics before the onset of a daughter‚Äôs risk of cutting. We addressed this limitation by

531

constructing community-level factors using data from ever-married women 20‚Äì49 years, an older

532

cohort than the one for which the FGMC outcome was observed. Still, if the composition of a

533

community changed substantially or if families moved between a daughter‚Äôs birth and the survey,

534

our community-level measures may have misrepresented the salient factors at the time of decision-

535

making about FGMC. This measurement error would tend to reduce the precision of estimates and

536

downwardly bias coefficients. Re-estimating final models without the oldest daughters (15‚Äì20 years)

537

confirmed that the relationship of community FGMC norms with a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC was

538

stronger. Thus, longitudinal surveys are needed to measure time-varying community characteristics

539

before the onset of a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC.

540

Second, the DHS sample cluster was a proxy for ‚Äúcommunity.‚Äù The DHS is not designed

541

for aggregation at the cluster level, and sample averages may produce imprecise estimates of true

542

cluster characteristics, especially when the number of women per cluster is small and the intra-class

543

correlation (ICCs) of the predictors is low (98). Depending on the characteristic being estimated,

544

sample means also may produce biased estimates if the degree of precision is related to other

545

community characteristics. In this sample, the mean cluster size was 24 ever-married women 20‚Äì49

546

years (range 1‚Äì127, SD 13). Estimated ICCs for community opportunities and community norms

547

were .30 and .27, respectively, above the .20-level at which bias becomes unacceptable for the

548

standard DHS cluster size of 25 respondents (98). Still, the average cluster size was small, so we

549

cannot state definitively the level of the bias of cluster-level estimates without conducting

550

simulations. Prior simulations have shown that the bias produced by using DHS sample clusters to

551

estimate community characteristics is small (98). Surveys intended for multilevel analyses should
26

medRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/19005520; this version posted October 18, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was
not certified by peer review) is the author/funder, who has granted medRxiv a license to display the preprint in perpetuity.
It is made available under a CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license .

552

draw separate within-community samples that are large enough to construct unbiased estimates of

553

community-level characteristics that are measured by aggregating individual responses (99).

554

Third, a conceptual concern with using the DHS sample cluster as the measure of

555

community is that the cluster may not capture the multiple social communities in which mother-

556

daughter pairs are embedded (38). Network, spatial, and qualitative data to measure women‚Äôs

557

overlapping activity spaces and social-group memberships would offer a more nuanced

558

understanding of the relevant socio-contextual influences on a daughter‚Äôs risk of cutting.

559

Fourth, our measure for women‚Äôs community-level opportunities relied on indicators of

560

schooling attainment, engagement in market work, and age at first residence with a spouse. These

561

measures are important but may not capture the range of women‚Äôs local opportunities, nor women‚Äôs

562

experience of opportunity. More contextualized community-level measures of women‚Äôs

563

opportunities, or empowerment, may influence more strongly a daughter‚Äôs risk of FGMC.

564

Fifth, the community-level exposures of interest were correlated, such that communities

565

tending to disfavor FGMC also tended to have more opportunities for women. Our theory of

566

gender systems predicted some correlation among these measures and their distinct influences; still,

567

distortion due to multi-collinearity may be a concern. In descriptive analyses, these community-level

568

exposures had an estimated Pearson pairwise correlation of 0.64; however, in models using only one

569

of the two community-level exposures, standard errors for the two community measures changed

570

very little in these models, suggesting that collinearity was not a major problem. Thus, these

571

constructs arguably were correlated but distinct components of a community gender system.

572

Finally, our analysis focused on two levels‚Äìthe mother-daughter pair and the community.

573

Yet, the influences of regional or national conditions warrant consideration. In sensitivity analyses,

574

results for primary explanatory variables were robust to including region fixed effects in our final

575

model. Since our primary interest was in community norms, community opportunity structures, and

576

cross-level interactions, we retained the more parsimonious model without regional dummies. In

577

future analyses, comparing individual, community, and national influences on a daughter‚Äôs FGMC

578

risk would reflect a comprehensive ecological model of change.

579

Implications for policies and programs

580

Our findings have several implications for policies and programs. Community-level

581

opposition to FGMC had a direct association with daughter‚Äôs FGMC risk and strengthened the

582

protective effect of maternal opposition to FGMC. This finding corroborates evidence from

583

TOSTAN, a quasi-experimental intervention study of the relationship between a human-rights-

584

based community empowerment and educational curriculum and girls‚Äô risk of experiencing FGMC.

585

In TOSTAN, communities were engaged in participatory dialogues and community-based activities

586

grounded in the principles of human rights, democratic processes, and women‚Äôs health, among other

587

topics. At the completion of these dialogues, communities deliberated and spearheaded collective

588

decisions about abandoning harmful practices, including FGMC, and engaged neighboring

589

communities to adopt the same new norms. The study showed evidence of diffusion of

590

abandonment from program participants to non-participants and more unfavorable attitudes about

591

FGMC and lower prevalence of cutting girls in intervention than comparison areas (100). Thus,

592

interventions aimed at changing collective perceptions of FGMC‚Äîsuch as community pledges to

593

reject FGMC‚Äîmay have multiplicative impacts on daughters‚Äô outcomes.

594

Community-level opportunities for women were not directly associated with daughter‚Äôs

595

FGMC risk, but our results imply that improving opportunities for women may reduce the risk of

596

FGMC among daughters whose mothers oppose the practice. Thus, future community-based

597

interventions might consider joint activities that involve informal educational opportunities and

598

training in income-generating activities for mothers. Future research should explore the joint

599

influence of community FGMC norms among men on the risk of FGMC among daughters and
28

medRxiv preprint doi: https://doi.org/10.1101/19005520; this version posted October 18, 2019. The copyright holder for this preprint (which was

It is made available under a CC-BY-NC-ND 4.0 International license .

600

whether engaging men in community-based programs would help to accelerate the abandonment of

601

FGMC. Going forward, practitioners should consider intervention packages that address community

602

gender norms specific to FGMC and that provide enabling resources to mothers, which empower

603

them collectively to imagine alternatives to dependence on marriage in the life course trajectories of

604

their daughters. Any effort to shift gender norms and opportunity structures will be most effective if

605

it attempts to respect the values of local communities.

606

Despite the importance of community context, we also found that maternal opposition to

607

FGMC was protective for daughters at most ages, even in communities that strongly opposed

608

change. Thus, even where community-level interventions are infeasible, interventions focused on the

609

beliefs of individuals may support change. Individual-level interventions might include provider

610

support for non-cutting during clinic visits, or one-on-one support from outreach workers or

611

religious leaders.

612

Conclusion

613

Results provide good support for a gender-systems theory of the multilevel influences on

614

FGMC. Integrated, multilevel interventions that address community-level gender norms about

615

FGMC, community-level opportunities for women outside the family, and personal beliefs about

616

FGMC among mothers of at-risk daughters may be needed for sustainable declines in the practice.
