Brief Summary

30

In this manuscript, we have studied the potential of retinal vessel density as

31

measured by optical coherence tomography angiography (OCT-A), as a biomarker

32

for detection of high-risk of developing exudative age-related macular degeneration

33

(AMD).

Abstract

36

Purpose

37

To determine whether vessel density (VD) as measured by optical coherence tomography

38

angiography (OCT-A) provide insights into retinal and choriocapillaris vascular changes with

39

ageing and intermediate dry age related macular degeneration (AMD).

40

Methods

41

Seventy-five participants were recruited into three cohorts; young healthy (YH) group, old healthy

42

(OH) and those at high-risk for exudative AMD. Raw OCT and OCT-A data from TOPCON DRI

43

OCT Triton were exported using Topcon IMAGENET 6.0 software, and 3D datasets were

44

analysed to determine retinal thickness and vessel density.

45

Results

46

Central macular thickness measurements revealed a trend of overall retinal thinning with

47

increasing age. VD through the full thickness of the retina was highest in ETDRS sector 4 (the

48

inferior macula) in all the cohorts. Mean VD was significantly higher in the deep capillary plexus

49

than the superficial capillary plexus in all ETDRS sectors in all cohorts but there was no

50

significant difference noted between groups. Choriocapillaris VD was significantly lower in all

51

ETDRS sectors in the in the AMD group compared with the YH and the OH groups.

52

Conclusions

53

Retinal vessel density maps, derived from the retinal plexi are not reliable biomarkers for

54

assessing the ageing macular. Our non-proprietary analysis of the vascular density of the

55

choriocapillaris revealed a significant drop off of VD with age and disease but further work is

56

required to corroborate this finding. If repeatable, choriocapillaris VD may provide a non-invasive

57

biomarker of healthy ageing and disease.

Introduction

59

Currently we lack a reliable, non-invasive vascular biomarker to monitor both the ‚Äúhealthy‚Äù

60

ageing and disease progression of age related macular degeneration (AMD). Optical

61

coherence tomography angiography (OCT-A) represents a novel, non-invasive, dye-less

62

retinal vascular imaging technique that can be rapidly acquired during a clinical consultation.

63

OCT-A is a progression of OCT, using repeated B-scans to detect motion contrast1. Based

64

on the assumption that this represents the movement of erythrocytes travelling through the

65

retinal blood vessels, the retinal vasculature can therefore be visualised.2 Previous studies

66

have shown the value of this imaging technique in the evaluation of glaucoma, diabetic

67

retinopathy (DR), retinal vein occlusions and AMD3.

68

The laminar structure of the retina lends itself to segmentation analysis. Histologically, the

69

retina has four retinal capillary plexuses, within the macular only three of these are

70

considered; the superficial, deep and intermediate capillary plexuses4, 5. When the macular

71

vasculature is imaged using OCT-A the vascular layers are segmented automatically by the

72

software. This results in the merging of the intermediate with the deep capillary plexus 3. The

73

regions of interest identified on the OCT-A therefore include the superficial capillary plexus

74

(SCP) and the deep capillary plexus (DCP)3. The SCP consists of the vasculature within the

75

retinal nerve fibre layer (RNFL) and ganglion cell layer (GCL). The DCP represents the

76

vascular plexuses present at two locations: These plexuses are at the border of the inner

77

nuclear layer and the outer plexiform layer, and the border of the inner plexiform layer and

78

inner nuclear layer6.

79

OCT-A images can be subjectively appraised for the presence of disease or post processed

80

to produce quantitative data that can be evaluated objectively. A number of studies7, 8 have

81

used perfusion indices such as vessel density and flow index9 as a method of quantitatively

82

analysing OCT-A images. Vessel density is defined as the ‚Äúpercentage area occupied by

83

vessels in the segmented area‚Äù9.Flow index is defined as ‚Äúthe average decorrelation values

84

in the segmented area‚Äù9. It has been proposed that these indices could be of value in

85

monitoring disease progression in AMD and DR7, 10, 11. When measuring vessel density, both

86

vessel length and diameter need to be considered. Poor perfusion can also result in vessel

87

dilation, therefore vessel density may not give complete information about the retinal

88

vascular status12.

89

There are a wide range of OCT-A devices available, each using a variety of algorithms to

90

capture retinal images. These include OCT-based optical micro-angiography (OMAG); split-

91

spectrum amplitude decorrelation angiography (SSADA); OCT angiography ratio analysis

92

(OCT-ARA); speckle variance; phase variance; and correlation mapping13. Specific OCT-A

93

models such as the AngioVue (Optovue, Inc., Fremont, CA, USA) have inbuilt software for

94

perfusion indices calculation14. However, the majority of studies have applied post image

95

analysis in a variety of methods after the images are exported from the OCT-A instrument2.

96

This heterogeneity in the image capture and analysis means that data calculated using

97

different algorithms are not directly comparable, due to systemic difference and poor

98

agreement2. Therefore, external normative databases are unreliable and longitudinal

99

monitoring of disease using vessel density requires scans to be performed using the same

100

instrument, scan location and algorithm2.

101

The aims of this study are to compare the macular vessel density values for; young healthy,

102

old healthy, and patients with intermediate dry age related macular degeneration at high risk

103

of progression to exudative AMD, and to determine if these perfusion indices could usefully

104

serve as a biomarker for both ‚Äúhealthy‚Äù ageing and the identification of disease risk.

Materials and Methods

111

Seventy-five participants were recruited through Auckland Optometry Clinic and Milford Eye

112

Clinic, Auckland, New Zealand. All participants provided written informed consent prior to

113

imaging. Ethics approval (#018241) from the University of Auckland Human Participants Ethics

114

Committee was obtained for this study. This research adhered to the tenets of the Declaration of

115

Helsinki.

116

Participants were divided into three groups, young healthy (YH) group, old healthy (OH) group

117

and the high-risk intermediate AMD group. Twenty participants were recruited into the YH group,

118

twenty-one were recruited into the OH group, and thirty-four recruited into the AMD group. A

119

comprehensive eye exam was conducted on each participant prior to the OCT and OCT-A scans

120

in order to determine high contrast BCVA and the ocular health status of the fundus. Patients

121

with any posterior eye disease that could potentially affect the choroidal or retinal vasculature

122

including, but not limited to glaucoma, polypoidal choroidal vasculopathy, DR, hypertensive

123

retinopathy, and high myopia (‚â•6D), were excluded from the study. Patients with any history of

124

neurological disorders were also excluded. The Young Healthy group consisted entirely of

125

individuals between the ages of 20 and 26 and a best corrected visual acuity of ‚â•6/9 in the eye

126

under test. The ‚ÄúOld Healthy‚Äù group consisted of individuals over the age of 55 years and who

127

had and a best corrected visual acuity of ‚â•6/9 in the eye under test. The ‚ÄúAMD group‚Äù consisted

128

entirely of patients with high risk intermediate dry AMD. This being diagnosed if the individual

129

had at least two of the following risk factors; reticular pseudodrusen, established neovascular

130

AMD in the fellow eye, confluent soft drusen with accompanying changes within the retinal

131

pigment epithelium. In order to ensure that all patients in the ‚ÄúAMD cohort‚Äù could maintain

132

fixation during OCT-A imaging, only those patients with a best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) of

133

6/15 or better were enrolled. The mean age of the participants in the YH group, OH and AMD

134

participants were 23 ¬± 3, 65 ¬± 10 and 75 ¬± 8 years respectively. Only one eye of each patient

135

was used for the analysis, and if the patient had both eyes scanned, the OCT-A scan that was

136

the better quality (assessed subjectively by the clinical grader) of the two was used. Mean best-

137

corrected visual acuity for the YH, OH and AMD group were 6/6, 6/9, and 6/12 respectively.

Data collection protocol

143

Participants were dilated with 1.0% tropicamide if the pupils were deemed to be too small for

144

adequate OCT scans. Intraocular pressures were measured before and after dilation by iCare

145

tonometry. OCT/OCT-A scans, fundus photography and clinical measurements were all

146

performed at the University of Auckland Optometry Clinic, in a single session.

148

SS-OCT-A device and scanning protocol

149

The swept source OCT-A device (Topcon DRI OCT Triton, Topcon Corporation, Japan) was

150

used to obtain the OCT and OCT-A scans. A macular line and 3x3mm2 OCT, and 3x3mm2 OCT-

151

A scan were performed on each participant. All scans were cantered on the fovea, and retinal

152

layers were identified using the IMAGENET 6.0 automated layer detection tool so that the

153

superficial (SCP) and deep (DCP) capillary plexuses could be evaluated. The quality of the

154

generated layers were checked manually. Scan quality was evaluated at the time of acquisition,

155

and repeated if required.

Quantitative analysis of OCT-A Images

158

Raw OCT and OCT-A data were exported using Topcon IMAGEnet 6.0 software. Since OCT-A

159

images are only qualitative, we chose to normalize the OCT-A datasets, prior to further analysis.

160

These datasets were then correlated with vessel density in each retinal sector. OCT-A 3D

161

datasets were analysed in three different ways (outlined below), to investigate the retinal and

162

choroidal thickness and vessel density (VD).

163
164

165

The nine Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study (ETDRS) sectors were computationally

166

generated and centred manually by the clinical examiner, on the fovea (using enface OCT-A) for

2. SCP, DCP and Choriocapillaris

184

Using the same ETDRS regions as above, the VD was extracted from layer 1 and 2 for the SCP

186

VD from the SCP, DCP and choriocapillaris was then extracted for each participant, in the three

187

In addition to OCT-A data, we also recorded the foveal thickness and subfoveal choroidal

Discussion

254

Although a number of changes within the ageing macular have previously been reported, a

255

reliable, non-invasive biomarker of both ‚Äúhealthy‚Äù macular ageing and disease risk remains

256

elusive. Retinal thickness, both at the fovea and extra-foveal regions, has previously been shown

257

to differ significantly between individuals with early AMD and age-matched controls with the

258

retina being thinner in individuals with disease 16.The results of our study also revealed that there

259

was a trend for the overall retinal thickness to thin with age but this difference was not

260

statistically significant between cohorts. It is also recognised that the choroid tends to thin with

261

age; with the nasal area being thinnest, and the subfoveal region being thickest17. In addition to

262

thinning with ‚Äúnormal ageing,‚Äù Choroidal thinning has also been demonstrated in individuals with

263

early AMD compared to age matched controls 16, 18-20 however, the significance of this

264

observation is controversial as both the extent and the pattern of thinning is significant in some20

265

but not all studies

266

contradictory with some studies showing reduction in late AMD21 and others showing no

267

change19; moreover, the changes in the vasculature of the choriocapillaris in early AMD that

268

have been reported from histological studies 19, 21 appear to be independent of choroidal

269

thickness.

270

Similarly inconclusive observations have been reported with respect to the width of the Foveal

271

avascular zone with age. Whilst some studies report no difference with age22, 23 others have

272

found that the FAZ enlarges with increasing age 24, 25. Where reported, the FA at the level of the

273

SCP has been found to be significantly smaller in >60 year olds compared to younger study

274

groups 14, but the significant of this difference is unknown and furthermore no difference was

275

observed when the FAZ at the level of the DCP was analysed14. Recently, a study studying the

276

FAZ in patients with intermediate non-exudative AMD found no significant difference in the width

277

of the FAZ between patients with non-exudative AMD and healthy controls 26. We likewise found

278

there to be no statically significant difference in the diameter of the FAZ between any of the 3

279

cohorts studied.

280

281

develop a reliable, non-invasive vascular biomarker of the ageing macular. The proprietary
